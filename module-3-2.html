Understanding the Controllable Canonical Form

The provided notes demonstrate how to convert a system from a general state-space representation into a standard structure known as the Controllable Canonical Form (CCF). This is done by first finding the system's Transfer Function, extracting its coefficients, and mapping them directly to the CCF matrices. Here is the step-by-step breakdown:

Step 1 Identify the Initial State-Space System

The given system is represented by the standard state-space equations \(\dot{x} = Ax + Bu\) and \(y = Cx + Du\). From the notes, we extract the matrices:

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}, \quad B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad C = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}, \quad D = [0] $$

Step 2 Calculate the Transfer Function

To find the canonical form easily, we first convert the system into a transfer function \(G(s)\) using the formula:

$$ G(s) = C(sI - A)^{-1}B + D $$

The term \((sI - A)\) subtracts matrix \(A\) from a diagonal matrix of \(s\) values. Taking its inverse and multiplying by \(C\) and \(B\) yields the transfer function. The notes skip the intermediate matrix multiplication and arrive at the result:

$$ G(s) = \frac{4s - 1}{s^2 - s - 2} $$

Step 3 Extract Coefficients by Inspection

A standard second-order transfer function has the form:

$$ G(s) = \frac{b_0 s^2 + b_1 s + b_2}{s^2 + a_1 s + a_2} $$

By comparing our calculated transfer function \( \frac{0 \cdot s^2 + 4s - 1}{s^2 - s - 2} \) with the standard form, we can identify the coefficients:

  • Denominator coefficients (poles): \(a_1 = -1\), \(a_2 = -2\)
  • Numerator coefficients (zeros): \(b_0 = 0\), \(b_1 = 4\), \(b_2 = -1\)

Step 4 Construct the Controllable Canonical Form

Once we have the coefficients, we can plug them directly into the standard Controllable Canonical Form templates for the new state matrices.

The New State Matrix (\(A_c\)) and Input Matrix (\(B_c\))

The \(A_c\) matrix uses the negative denominator coefficients in the bottom row. The \(B_c\) matrix is always a standard vector ending in 1.

$$ \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x}_1 \\ \dot{x}_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -a_2 & -a_1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} u $$

Substituting \(a_1 = -1\) and \(a_2 = -2\):

$$ \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x}_1 \\ \dot{x}_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} u $$

The New Output Matrix (\(C_c\))

The \(C_c\) matrix is built using a combination of the numerator and denominator coefficients:

$$ y = \begin{bmatrix} b_2 - a_2 b_0 & b_1 - a_1 b_0 \end{bmatrix} $$

Since \(b_0 = 0\), the terms \(a_2 b_0\) and \(a_1 b_0\) become zero, leaving us with just \(b_2\) and \(b_1\):

$$ y = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 4 \end{bmatrix} x $$

Understanding the Sampling Theorem

1 What Your Notes Say

State and explain sampling theorem (3)

"A band limited continuous time signal with highest frequency \(f_m\) hertz can be uniquely recovered from its samples provided that the sampling rate \(F_s\) is greater than or equal to \(2f_m\) samples per seconds."

"During sampling process, a continuous-time signal is converted into discrete-time signals by taking samples of continuous-time signal at discrete time intervals."

$$ x(nT_s) = x(t) $$ $$ T = \text{Sampling Interval}, \quad x(t) = \text{Analog input signal} $$

2 The Simple English Explanation

Imagine you are watching a bird flap its wings. In real life, the movement is perfectly smooth and continuous. This is an analog (continuous-time) signal.

Now, imagine you want to record this bird using a digital camera. The camera doesn't capture smooth reality; it takes a series of rapid photographs (frames). This process of taking snapshots is called sampling. You are converting smooth reality into a sequence of dots or pictures (a discrete-time signal).

The Big Question: How fast do you need to take pictures?

If the bird flaps its wings 10 times a second (this is \(f_m\), the highest frequency), how many photos do you need to take per second so that when you play them back, the motion looks correct?

  • If you take only 5 photos a second, you'll miss wing flaps. When you play it back, it might look like the bird is floating or flying upside down. (This error is called aliasing).
  • The Sampling Theorem (Nyquist Theorem) gives us the golden rule: You must take pictures at least TWICE as fast as the fastest movement.
  • So, if the wings flap 10 times a second (\(f_m = 10\)), you must take at least 20 photos per second (\(F_s \ge 20\)).

3 Breaking Down the Variables

\( f_m \)

Maximum Signal Frequency: The absolute highest pitch or fastest change occurring in your original analog signal (measured in Hertz).

\( F_s \)

Sampling Rate (or Frequency): How many "snapshots" you are taking per second. The theorem dictates \( F_s \ge 2f_m \).

\( T \) (or \( T_s \))

Sampling Interval: The time gap between each snapshot. It is the inverse of the sampling rate (\( T = 1/F_s \)).

\( x(nT_s) = x(t) \)

The Conversion Equation: This math simply says "Our digital samples \(x(nT_s)\) are just the exact values of the continuous wave \(x(t)\) captured at specific time steps (\(n\))."

Controllability and Observability

1 Formal Definitions

12.a) Define controllability and observability of a system

Controllability: A system is said to be completely state controllable if it is possible to transfer the system state from any initial state to any desired final state in a finite time interval by applying an unconstrained control input.

Observability: A system is said to be completely observable if the initial state can be completely determined from the knowledge of the output and input over a finite time interval.

2 The Simple English Explanation

Controllability = "Can I drive it there?"

Imagine you are driving a car. Controllability asks: "Using the steering wheel, gas pedal, and brakes (your inputs), can you steer the car from where it is right now to exactly where you want it to be within a specific amount of time?"

If the steering wheel is broken, the system is uncontrollable because no matter what you do, you can't force the car to go exactly where you want.

Observability = "Can I figure out where it started?"

Imagine you are tracking a rocket but you only have a radar measuring its altitude (your output). Observability asks: "Just by looking at the altitude graph over the last few minutes, can I calculate exactly what the rocket's original speed, position, and angle were?"

If a sensor is broken or doesn't give you enough unique information to figure out the internal workings, the system is unobservable.

Stability of Sampled Data Systems

1 The Problem

b) Check the stability of the sampled data control system shown below

$$ z^3 - 0.2z^2 - 0.25z + 0.05 = 0 $$

2 Solving with Jury Stability Criterion

To determine if a discrete-time (sampled) system is stable, we check if all the roots of its characteristic equation lie inside the unit circle (\(|z| < 1\)). We do this without calculating the actual roots by using the Jury Stability Test.

Step 1: Define the Coefficients

For a 3rd-order equation \( P(z) = a_3z^3 + a_2z^2 + a_1z + a_0 = 0 \), our coefficients are:

  • \( a_3 = 1 \)
  • \( a_2 = -0.2 \)
  • \( a_1 = -0.25 \)
  • \( a_0 = 0.05 \)

Step 2: Check Initial Conditions

A stable 3rd-order system must pass these three quick mathematical checks:

  1. \( P(1) > 0 \):
    \( P(1) = 1 - 0.2 - 0.25 + 0.05 = 0.6 \) ✓ (0.6 > 0)
  2. \( (-1)^n P(-1) > 0 \) (Since \(n=3\), this means \( P(-1) < 0 \)):
    \( P(-1) = (-1)^3 - 0.2(-1)^2 - 0.25(-1) + 0.05 = -1 - 0.2 + 0.25 + 0.05 = -0.9 \) ✓ (-0.9 < 0)
  3. \( |a_0| < a_3 \):
    \( |0.05| < 1 \) ✓ (0.05 < 1)

Step 3: The Jury Table

Since it's a 3rd-order equation, we need to build one set of determinant values (\(b_0\) and \(b_2\)) from the coefficients and check if \( |b_0| > |b_2| \).

Row \(z^0\) \(z^1\) \(z^2\) \(z^3\)
1 0.05 (\(a_0\)) -0.25 (\(a_1\)) -0.2 (\(a_2\)) 1 (\(a_3\))
2 1 (\(a_3\)) -0.2 (\(a_2\)) -0.25 (\(a_1\)) 0.05 (\(a_0\))

Calculating the determinants for Row 3:

$$ b_0 = \begin{vmatrix} 0.05 & 1 \\ 1 & 0.05 \end{vmatrix} = (0.05)(0.05) - (1)(1) = 0.0025 - 1 = -0.9975 $$ $$ b_2 = \begin{vmatrix} 0.05 & -0.25 \\ 1 & -0.2 \end{vmatrix} = (0.05)(-0.2) - (1)(-0.25) = -0.01 + 0.25 = 0.24 $$

Checking the final magnitude condition:

\( |-0.9975| > |0.24| \implies 0.9975 > 0.24 \) ✓ Satisfied

Conclusion: Because all of the Jury Stability conditions are satisfied, all roots lie inside the unit circle. The sampled data control system is STABLE.

Solving a Homogeneous State Equation

1 The Problem

Compute the solution to the state equation:

$$ \dot{x} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix}x, \quad x(0) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

2 Step-by-Step Solution

This is an unforced (homogeneous) state equation of the form \( \dot{x}(t) = Ax(t) \). Because there is no input vector \( u(t) \), the general solution relies entirely on the initial state and the system's natural dynamics. The formula for the solution is:

$$ x(t) = e^{At} x(0) $$

Step 1: Find the State Transition Matrix (\( e^{At} \))

Normally, calculating the matrix exponential \( e^{At} \) requires finding the inverse Laplace transform of \( (sI - A)^{-1} \). However, there is a special shortcut when matrix \(A\) is a diagonal matrix (only the main diagonal has non-zero numbers).

For a diagonal matrix, \( e^{At} \) is simply a new diagonal matrix where you apply \( e^{\lambda t} \) to each diagonal element:

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \implies e^{At} = \begin{bmatrix} e^{-1t} & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-2t} \end{bmatrix} $$

Step 2: Multiply by Initial Conditions

Now, we just multiply our State Transition Matrix by the initial state vector \( x(0) \) to find \( x(t) \).

$$ x(t) = \begin{bmatrix} e^{-t} & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-2t} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Performing the row-by-column multiplication:

$$ x(t) = \begin{bmatrix} (e^{-t} \cdot 1) + (0 \cdot 1) \\ (0 \cdot 1) + (e^{-2t} \cdot 1) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} e^{-t} \\ e^{-2t} \end{bmatrix} $$

Similarity Transformation to CCF

1 The Problem

a) Convert the system into controllable canonical form by applying similarity transformation.

$$ \dot{x} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix}x + \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}u, \quad y = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}x $$

2 Step-by-Step Solution

Step 1: Find the Controllability Matrix (\(Q_c\))

The controllability matrix is defined as \( Q_c = \begin{bmatrix} B & AB \end{bmatrix} \).

$$ AB = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ Q_c = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 2 & -4 \end{bmatrix} $$

Step 2: Find the Transformation Matrix (\(M\))

First, we need the characteristic equation of \(A\) to find the \(W\) matrix. \( |sI - A| = 0 \):

$$ \begin{vmatrix} s+1 & 0 \\ 0 & s+2 \end{vmatrix} = (s+1)(s+2) = s^2 + 3s + 2 = 0 $$

The coefficients are \(a_1 = 3\) and \(a_2 = 2\). The matrix \(W\) for a 2nd-order system is constructed using \(a_1\):

$$ W = \begin{bmatrix} a_1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} $$

Now compute \( M = Q_c W \):

$$ M = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 2 & -4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix} $$

Step 3: Calculate the New Canonical Matrices

We need \(M^{-1}\) to transform the system. \( |M| = (2)(2) - (1)(2) = 2 \).

$$ M^{-1} = \frac{1}{2} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -2 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -0.5 \\ -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Now apply the similarity transformations: \( A_c = M^{-1}AM \), \( B_c = M^{-1}B \), and \( C_c = CM \).

$$ A_c = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -0.5 \\ -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -2 & -3 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ B_c = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -0.5 \\ -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ C_c = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 3 \end{bmatrix} $$

Stability via Routh-Hurwitz

1 The Problem

b) Determine the stability of the system.

$$ \dot{x} = \begin{bmatrix} -3 & 7 & 9 \\ 2 & 1 & 3 \\ -4 & -3 & -8 \end{bmatrix}x + \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}u, \quad y = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}x $$

2 Step-by-Step Solution

Step 1: Find the Characteristic Equation

Stability is determined by the eigenvalues of the system matrix \(A\), found by solving \( |sI - A| = 0 \).

$$ |sI - A| = \begin{vmatrix} s+3 & -7 & -9 \\ -2 & s-1 & -3 \\ 4 & 3 & s+8 \end{vmatrix} = 0 $$

Expanding the determinant yields the characteristic polynomial:

$$ s^3 + 10s^2 + 44s - 7 = 0 $$

Step 2: Construct the Routh Array

We use the Routh-Hurwitz criterion to check if any roots lie in the right-half plane (causing instability).

\(s^3\)144
\(s^2\)10-7
\(s^1\)44.70
\(s^0\)-7

Calculation for \(s^1\) row: \( \frac{(10 \times 44) - (1 \times -7)}{10} = \frac{447}{10} = 44.7 \)

Conclusion: Looking at the first column of the Routh array (1, 10, 44.7, -7), there is exactly one sign change (from +44.7 to -7). This indicates there is one pole in the right-half s-plane, meaning the system is UNSTABLE.

Pole Placement (State Feedback)

1 The Problem

a) Design a state feedback controller for the system to place the eigenvalues of the closed loop system matrix at \(-2 \pm j2\).

$$\dot{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix}x + \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}u, \quad y = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}x$$

2 Step-by-Step Solution

Step 1: Find the Desired Characteristic Equation

The desired closed-loop poles are given as \(s = -2 \pm j2\). The characteristic equation for these poles is \( (s - \mu_1)(s - \mu_2) = 0 \).

$$(s - (-2 + j2))(s - (-2 - j2)) = (s + 2 - j2)(s + 2 + j2) = 0$$ $$(s + 2)^2 - (j2)^2 = s^2 + 4s + 4 - (-4) = 0$$
$$\text{Desired Eq: } s^2 + 4s + 8 = 0$$

Step 2: Find the Closed-Loop System Equation

With state feedback \( u = -Kx \) where \( K = \begin{bmatrix} k_1 & k_2 \end{bmatrix} \), the new system matrix becomes \( (A - BK) \).

$$A - BK = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} k_1 & k_2 \end{bmatrix}$$ $$A - BK = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} k_1 & k_2 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 - k_1 & 1 - k_2 \\ 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Now, find its characteristic equation \( |sI - (A - BK)| = 0 \):

$$\begin{vmatrix} s - 1 + k_1 & -1 + k_2 \\ -2 & s - 2 \end{vmatrix} = 0$$ $$(s - 1 + k_1)(s - 2) - (-2)(-1 + k_2) = 0$$ $$(s^2 - 2s - s + 2 + k_1s - 2k_1) + (-2 + 2k_2) = 0$$
$$\text{Actual Eq: } s^2 + (k_1 - 3)s + (2k_2 - 2k_1) = 0$$

Step 3: Compare Coefficients & Solve

Equate the coefficients of the "Desired" and "Actual" characteristic equations:

  • For \(s^1\):
    \( k_1 - 3 = 4 \implies \mathbf{k_1 = 7} \)
  • For \(s^0\):
    \( 2k_2 - 2k_1 = 8 \)
    Substituting \(k_1 = 7\): \( 2k_2 - 14 = 8 \implies 2k_2 = 22 \implies \mathbf{k_2 = 11} \)
$$\text{Feedback Gain Matrix } K = \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 11 \end{bmatrix}$$

State Space to Transfer Function

1 The Problem

b) Derive the transfer function of the system when the initial state is zero.

$$\dot{x} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ -3 & -2 \end{bmatrix}x + \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}u, \quad y = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}x$$

2 Step-by-Step Solution

Step 1: Use the Transfer Function Formula

The general formula to convert a state-space model to a transfer function \(G(s)\) is:

$$G(s) = C(sI - A)^{-1}B + D$$

Here, \(D = 0\), so we just need to calculate \( C(sI - A)^{-1}B \).

Step 2: Find \((sI - A)^{-1}\)

$$sI - A = \begin{bmatrix} s & 0 \\ 0 & s \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ -3 & -2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} s+1 & -1 \\ 3 & s+2 \end{bmatrix}$$

To find the inverse, we swap the main diagonal, negate the anti-diagonal, and divide by the determinant:

$$\text{Determinant} = (s+1)(s+2) - (-1)(3) = s^2 + 3s + 2 + 3 = s^2 + 3s + 5$$
$$(sI - A)^{-1} = \frac{1}{s^2 + 3s + 5} \begin{bmatrix} s+2 & 1 \\ -3 & s+1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 3: Multiply by B and C

First, multiply the inverse matrix by the input vector \(B\):

$$(sI - A)^{-1}B = \frac{1}{s^2 + 3s + 5} \begin{bmatrix} s+2 & 1 \\ -3 & s+1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{s^2 + 3s + 5} \begin{bmatrix} s+2 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix}$$

Finally, multiply the result by the output vector \(C\):

$$G(s) = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \frac{1}{s^2 + 3s + 5} \begin{bmatrix} s+2 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix}$$
$$G(s) = \frac{-3}{s^2 + 3s + 5}$$

Transfer Function to Canonical Form

1 The Problem

Consider a system with a transfer function:

$$ \frac{Y(s)}{U(s)} = \frac{10}{(s+1)(s+5)} $$

Obtain the state space model of the system in a canonical form. Find the Eigenvalues of the system matrix.

2 Step-by-Step Solution

Step 1: Expand the Transfer Function

First, multiply out the terms in the denominator to get the standard polynomial form:

$$ G(s) = \frac{10}{(s+1)(s+5)} = \frac{10}{s^2 + 6s + 5} $$

Step 2: Map to Controllable Canonical Form

Compare the expanded transfer function to the standard form \( \frac{b_0s^2 + b_1s + b_2}{s^2 + a_1s + a_2} \):

  • Denominator coefficients: \( a_1 = 6, \quad a_2 = 5 \)
  • Numerator coefficients: \( b_0 = 0, \quad b_1 = 0, \quad b_2 = 10 \)

Using the standard controllable canonical form templates, we construct the state and output matrices:

$$ \dot{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -a_2 & -a_1 \end{bmatrix}x + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}u \implies \dot{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -5 & -6 \end{bmatrix}x + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}u $$
$$ y = \begin{bmatrix} b_2 & b_1 \end{bmatrix}x \implies y = \begin{bmatrix} 10 & 0 \end{bmatrix}x $$

Step 3: Find the Eigenvalues

The eigenvalues of the system matrix \(A\) are simply the roots of the characteristic equation, which is identical to the denominator of the transfer function.

$$ |sI - A| = s^2 + 6s + 5 = 0 $$ $$ (s + 1)(s + 5) = 0 $$

Solving for \(s\):

$$ \lambda_1 = -1, \quad \lambda_2 = -5 $$
