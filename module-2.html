1 Ziegler-Nichols PID Tuning Rules

FREQUENTLY ASKED May 2019Sep 2020Jul 2021

In 1942, Ziegler and Nichols described simple mathematical procedures (the first and second methods) for tuning PID controllers. These empirical procedures are standard in control systems practice. They make a priori assumptions on the system model but do not require the mathematical model to be specifically known.

1.1 The First Method (Step Response Method)

The first method is applied to plants with open-loop step responses that exhibit an S-shaped curve. This is typical of a first-order system with transportation delay, or a plant made up of a series of first-order systems.

S-Shaped Response Curve

The response is characterized by two parameters: L (Delay Time) and T (Time Constant). These are found by drawing a tangent to the step response at its point of inflection and noting its intersections with the time axis and the steady-state value line. The approximated plant model is:

$$ G(s) = \frac{K e^{-Ls}}{Ts + 1} $$

Ziegler-Nichols Recipe - First Method:

PID Type \( K_p \) \( T_i = K_p / K_i \) \( T_d = K_d / K_p \)
P \( \frac{T}{L} \) \( \infty \) 0
PI \( 0.9 \frac{T}{L} \) \( \frac{L}{0.3} \) 0
PID \( 1.2 \frac{T}{L} \) \( 2L \) \( 0.5L \)

Note: The PID controller tuned by this method yields: \( G_c(s) = 0.6T \frac{(s + 1/L)^2}{s} \)

1.2 The Second Method (Ultimate Cycle Method)

The second method targets plants that can be rendered unstable (marginally stable) under purely proportional control. The technique is designed to result in a closed-loop system with approximately 25% overshoot.

Experimental Tuning Steps:

  1. Reduce the integrator and derivative gains to 0 (\( T_i = \infty \) and \( T_d = 0 \)). Use only proportional feedback control.
  2. Increase \( K_p \) from 0 to a critical value \( K_{cr} \) at which sustained, continuous oscillations first occur. (If the system does not exhibit sustained oscillations for any \( K_p \), this method cannot be used).
  3. Note the critical gain \( K_{cr} \) and the corresponding period of sustained oscillation, \( P_{cr} \).

Sustained Oscillations at Critical Gain

Ziegler-Nichols Recipe - Second Method:

PID Type \( K_p \) \( T_i \) \( T_d \)
P \( 0.5 K_{cr} \) \( \infty \) 0
PI \( 0.45 K_{cr} \) \( \frac{P_{cr}}{1.2} \) 0
PID \( 0.6 K_{cr} \) \( 0.5 P_{cr} \) \( 0.125 P_{cr} \)
UNIVERSITY QP (MAY 2019)

Solved Problem: Ziegler-Nichols Analytical Tuning

Question: A unity feedback system has the plant transfer function \( G(s) = \frac{6}{(s+1)(s+2)(s+3)} \). Tune the PID controller which is to be placed in series with the plant using the Ziegler-Nichols method.

Step 1: Determine Critical Gain (\(K_{cr}\)) using Routh Array

Set \( T_i = \infty \) and \( T_d = 0 \). The system uses only proportional control \( K_p \). The characteristic equation is \( 1 + K_p G(s) = 0 \).

$$ 1 + \frac{6 K_p}{(s+1)(s+2)(s+3)} = 0 $$ $$ (s+1)(s^2 + 5s + 6) + 6K_p = 0 $$ $$ s^3 + 6s^2 + 11s + 6 + 6K_p = 0 $$

Construct the Routh Array:

\( s^3 \)111
\( s^2 \)6\( 6 + 6K_p \)
\( s^1 \)\( \frac{66 - (6 + 6K_p)}{6} = 10 - K_p \)0
\( s^0 \)\( 6 + 6K_p \)0

For marginal stability (sustained oscillations), the \( s^1 \) row must be zero.

$$ 10 - K_p = 0 \implies K_{cr} = 10 $$

Step 2: Determine Ultimate Period (\(P_{cr}\))

Substitute \( K_p = 10 \) into the auxiliary equation from the \( s^2 \) row:

$$ 6s^2 + (6 + 6(10)) = 0 $$ $$ 6s^2 + 66 = 0 \implies s^2 = -11 $$ $$ s = \pm j\sqrt{11} $$

The frequency of sustained oscillations \( \omega_{cr} \) is \( \sqrt{11} \) rad/sec.

$$ P_{cr} = \frac{2\pi}{\omega_{cr}} = \frac{2\pi}{\sqrt{11}} \approx 1.895 \text{ seconds} $$

Step 3: Calculate PID Parameters

Using the Ziegler-Nichols table for a PID controller:

  • \( K_p = 0.6 K_{cr} = 0.6 \times 10 = \mathbf{6} \)
  • \( T_i = 0.5 P_{cr} = 0.5 \times 1.895 = \mathbf{0.9475} \)
  • \( T_d = 0.125 P_{cr} = 0.125 \times 1.895 = \mathbf{0.2368} \)

The tuned PID controller transfer function is:

$$ G_c(s) = 6 \left[ 1 + \frac{1}{0.9475s} + 0.2368s \right] $$

2 Root Locus Design Fundamentals

Unlike Bode plot design which relies on frequency domain specifications (Phase Margin, Gain Margin, Bandwidth), Root Locus design works directly with Time Domain specifications. Performance requirements such as Maximum Peak Overshoot (\( \%M_p \)), Settling Time (\( T_s \)), and Damping Ratio (\( \zeta \)) translate directly to specific locations on the s-plane.

Time Domain to s-Plane Mapping

1. Damping Ratio (\(\zeta\)): Derived from Peak Overshoot.

$$ \%M_p = e^{\frac{-\zeta\pi}{\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}}} \times 100 $$

2. Natural Frequency (\(\omega_n\)): Derived from Settling Time (2% criterion).

$$ T_s = \frac{4}{\zeta\omega_n} \implies \omega_n = \frac{4}{\zeta T_s} $$

The Dominant Closed-Loop Pole (\(S_d\))

Once \( \zeta \) and \( \omega_n \) are found, the desired dominant closed-loop pole location on the complex s-plane is fixed at:

$$ S_d = -\zeta\omega_n \pm j\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2} $$

Design Goal: The compensator \( G_c(s) \) must be designed such that the root locus of the compensated system \( G_c(s)G(s) \) passes exactly through this desired point \( S_d \).

The Core Principle of Root Locus Compensation

For \( S_d \) to lie on the root locus, it must satisfy the Angle Condition:

$$ \angle G_c(S_d)G(S_d) = \pm 180^\circ (2k+1) $$

If the uncompensated system \( G(s) \) does not pass through \( S_d \), there is an angle deficiency (\(\phi\)). A Lead Compensator is designed to provide exactly this missing angle \( \phi \) so the locus shifts to pass through \( S_d \).

3 Lead Compensator Design via Root Locus

ESSAY QUESTION (10 MARKS) May 2019May 2023

Step-by-Step Algorithm

1

Determine the Dominant Pole \( S_d \)

Extract \( \zeta \) and \( \omega_n \) from transient specifications (\% Overshoot, Settling Time). Calculate \( S_d = -\zeta\omega_n + j\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2} \).

2

Calculate the Angle Deficiency \( \phi \)

Connect all open-loop poles and zeros of \( G(s) \) to \( S_d \). Calculate the net angle contribution at \( S_d \).

$$ \phi = 180^\circ - \left( \sum \angle \text{Poles} - \sum \angle \text{Zeros} \right) $$

This \( \phi \) is the exact phase angle the lead compensator must contribute.

3

Locate Compensator Pole and Zero (The Bisector Method)

To find the exact coordinates of the compensator's pole (\(P_c\)) and zero (\(Z_c\)), we use a specific geometric construction on the s-plane:

  • Draw a horizontal line AP passing through \(S_d\), extending to the left (\(-\infty\)).
  • Draw a line PO connecting the origin to \(S_d\).
  • Draw the bisector PC of the angle \(\angle APO\).
  • Draw two lines from \(S_d\), making an angle of \(\phi/2\) on either side of the bisector PC.
  • The intersections of these two lines with the negative real axis give the locations of the Zero (\(Z_c\)) (closer to origin) and Pole (\(P_c\)) (further left).
4

Form the Compensator Transfer Function

$$ G_c(s) = \frac{s + Z_c}{s + P_c} = \frac{s + \frac{1}{T}}{s + \frac{1}{\alpha T}} $$
5

Determine Open-Loop Gain \( K \)

Apply the Magnitude Condition of the root locus to the fully compensated system \( G_c(s)G(s) \) evaluated at \( s = S_d \):

$$ K = \frac{\prod (\text{Distances from all poles to } S_d)}{\prod (\text{Distances from all zeros to } S_d)} $$

Root Locus Lead Compensator Design

Question: Design a lead compensator for a unity feedback system with open-loop transfer function \( G(s) = \frac{K}{s(s+8)} \) to satisfy the following specifications: (1) Percentage overshoot = 9.5%, (2) Natural frequency of oscillation = 12 rad/sec, (3) Velocity error constant \( K_v \ge 10 \).

Step 1: Determine the Dominant Pole, \(S_d\)

Given \( \omega_n = 12 \) rad/sec and \( \%M_p = 9.5\% \).

$$ \%M_p = e^{\frac{-\zeta\pi}{\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}}} \times 100 \implies e^{\frac{-\zeta\pi}{\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}}} = 0.095 $$ $$ \frac{-\zeta\pi}{\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}} = \ln(0.095) \approx -2.354 $$ $$ \frac{\zeta^2 \pi^2}{1-\zeta^2} = (-2.354)^2 = 5.54 $$ $$ \zeta^2 \pi^2 = 5.54 - 5.54\zeta^2 \implies \zeta^2(\pi^2 + 5.54) = 5.54 $$ $$ \zeta = \sqrt{\frac{5.54}{9.87 + 5.54}} \approx 0.6 $$

Calculate the location of the dominant pole:

$$ S_d = -\zeta\omega_n \pm j\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2} = -(0.6 \times 12) \pm j12\sqrt{1 - 0.6^2} = -7.2 \pm j9.6 $$

Step 2: Calculate Angle Deficiency, \(\phi\)

The uncompensated system has poles at \( s = 0 \) and \( s = -8 \). Calculate the angles from these poles to \( S_d = -7.2 + j9.6 \):

$$ \theta_1 (\text{from } s=0) = 180^\circ - \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{9.6}{7.2}\right) = 180^\circ - 53.1^\circ = 126.9^\circ $$ $$ \theta_2 (\text{from } s=-8) = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{9.6}{8 - 7.2}\right) = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{9.6}{0.8}\right) = 85.2^\circ $$

Net angle contribution of uncompensated system at \( S_d \):

$$ \angle G(S_d) = 0^\circ - (\theta_1 + \theta_2) = -(126.9^\circ + 85.2^\circ) = -212.1^\circ $$

To satisfy the root locus angle condition (-180°), the lead compensator must add \(\phi\):

$$ \phi = 180^\circ - 212.1^\circ = 32.1^\circ \text{ (QP uses } 32^\circ) $$

Step 3: Find Compensator Pole & Zero (Bisector Method)

Based on geometry (or applying trigonometric sine/cosine rules to the bisector construction):

  • \( \angle APO = 180^\circ - \theta_1 = 53.1^\circ \)
  • Bisector angle = \( 53.1^\circ / 2 = 26.55^\circ \)
  • \( \angle CPD = \angle BPC = \phi / 2 = 32^\circ / 2 = 16^\circ \)

Applying trigonometry to find the exact intercepts on the real axis yields (as per QP solution):

$$ \text{Zero, } Z_c = -9.1 $$ $$ \text{Pole, } P_c = -16.25 $$ $$ G_c(s) = \frac{s + 9.1}{s + 16.25} $$

Step 4: Determine Gain K & Verify

Apply the Magnitude Condition to \( G_c(s)G(s) \) at \( s = S_d \):

$$ |G_c(S_d)G(S_d)| = 1 \implies K \left| \frac{S_d + 9.1}{S_d(S_d + 8)(S_d + 16.25)} \right| = 1 $$ $$ K = \frac{|-7.2 + j9.6| \times |-7.2+j9.6+8| \times |-7.2+j9.6+16.25|}{|-7.2+j9.6+9.1|} $$ $$ K = \frac{12 \times 9.63 \times 13.13}{9.78} \approx 150 $$

Check the Velocity Error Constant (\( K_v \)):

$$ K_v = \lim_{s \to 0} s G_c(s)G(s) = \lim_{s \to 0} s \frac{150(s + 9.1)}{s(s + 8)(s + 16.25)} = \frac{150 \times 9.1}{8 \times 16.25} = 10.5 $$

Since 10.5 \(\ge\) 10, the design is acceptable.

4 Lag Compensator Design via Root Locus

A lag compensator is used when the system exhibits satisfactory transient response (dominant poles are correctly located) but unsatisfactory steady-state error characteristics. The goal is to increase the open-loop gain (improving \( K_p, K_v \)) without appreciably changing the root locus in the neighborhood of the dominant closed-loop poles.

The Angle Constraint

To avoid changing the root locus, the angle contribution of the lag network at \( S_d \) must be extremely small (typically \(\le 5^\circ\)). This is achieved by placing the compensator's pole and zero very close together, and very near the origin.

Step-by-Step Algorithm

1

Locate Dominant Poles

Draw the root locus for the uncompensated system \( G(s) \). Based on the transient specs, locate the dominant closed-loop poles \( S_d \) on the locus. Find the required gain \( K_{actual} \) to operate at this point.

2

Determine Required Gain Increase Factor (A)

Calculate the particular static error constant using \( K_{actual} \). Compare it to the desired static error constant to find the factor A:

$$ A = \frac{K_{desired}}{K_{actual}} $$
3

Determine Attenuation \( \beta \)

The lag compensator \( G_c(s) = \frac{s + 1/T}{s + 1/\beta T} \) provides a low-frequency gain of \( \beta \). To account for the slight shift in the root locus (which might drop the gain slightly), choose:

$$ \beta = (1.1 \text{ to } 1.2) \times A $$
4

Locate Zero (\( Z_c \)) and Pole (\( P_c \))

Place the zero of the compensator relatively close to the origin, specifically much smaller than the magnitude of the dominant pole, or a factor of 10-50 times smaller than the nearest system pole:

$$ Z_c = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{p_{nearest}}{10 \text{ to } 50} $$

Then, calculate the pole:

$$ P_c = \frac{1}{\beta T} = \frac{Z_c}{\beta} $$
5

Finalize Transfer Function

$$ G_c(s) = \frac{s + 1/T}{s + 1/\beta T} $$

Check if the steady-state error requirement is met. The root locus will pass very close to the original \( S_d \).
