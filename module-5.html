1 Characteristics of Nonlinear Systems

THEORY May 2019Sep 2020

Difference between Stability Analysis of Linear and Nonlinear Systems

  • Linear Systems: Stability is an absolute property of the system. It is completely independent of the initial conditions or the amplitude of the input. Evaluated easily using characteristic equation roots (poles).
  • Nonlinear Systems: Stability is highly dependent on the initial conditions and the amplitude of the input. A system may be stable for small inputs but unstable for large ones. They may also exhibit sustained oscillations (limit cycles). Analysis requires specialized techniques like Describing Functions, Phase Plane, or Lyapunov methods.

Unlike linear systems, nonlinear systems do not obey the principle of superposition (homogeneity and additivity). They exhibit several unique phenomena not found in linear systems. Five key characteristics are:

1. Jump Resonance

In the frequency response of nonlinear systems, the amplitude of the response may jump discontinuously from one point to another for smoothly decreasing or increasing values of input frequency. The frequency response curve tilts, causing a multi-valued region.

2. Limit Cycles

These are oscillations in the unforced (autonomous) response of nonlinear systems with a strictly fixed amplitude and frequency, independent of initial conditions. They represent isolated closed trajectories in the phase plane. (Can be Stable, Unstable, or Semi-stable).

3. Sub-harmonic Oscillations

When a nonlinear system is excited by a sinusoidal signal of frequency \(\omega\), the steady-state response may contain oscillations whose frequencies are an integral sub-multiple of the forcing frequency (e.g., \(\omega/2, \omega/3\)).

4. Frequency Entrainment

If a periodic input is applied to a system exhibiting a limit cycle, the limit cycle frequency \(\omega_L\) may be "captured" or entrained by the forcing frequency \(\omega_f\) within a certain band of frequencies.

5. Asynchronous Quenching

It is possible to entirely quench (suppress) a limit cycle oscillation by forcing the system with a periodic signal of a frequency \(\omega_q\), where \(\omega_L\) and \(\omega_q\) are not harmonically related to each other.

Detailed Analysis: Limit Cycles

What is a limit cycle? How will you determine stable and unstable limit cycle using phase portrait?

The response of nonlinear systems may exhibit oscillations with fixed amplitude and frequency; these oscillations are called limit cycles. Once the system trajectories converge to a limit cycle, it will continue to remain in the closed trajectory in the state space identified as limit cycles. In many systems the limit cycles are undesirable particularly when the amplitude is not small and result in some unwanted phenomena.

Different types of limit cycles are:

  • Stable Limit Cycle: All trajectories in the vicinity of the limit cycle converge to it as \( t \to \infty \).
  • Unstable Limit Cycle: All trajectories in the vicinity of the limit cycle diverge from it as \( t \to \infty \).
  • Semi-Stable Limit Cycle: Some of the trajectories in the vicinity of the limit cycle converge to it as \( t \to \infty \) (e.g., from the outside), while others diverge from it (e.g., from the inside).

3 Describing Function & Harmonic Linearization

CORE CONCEPT Apr 2018May 2019

Analysis through Harmonic Linearization

What is Harmonic Linearization? It is an approximation technique used to analyze nonlinear systems in the frequency domain. It consists of replacing a nonlinear element within a system by an "equivalent" linear time-invariant system (a variable gain), which is in a sense the best possible linear approximation of the given nonlinear system for a sinusoidal input.

When a sinusoidal signal \( x(t) = X \sin(\omega t) \) is passed through a nonlinear element, the output \( y(t) \) is generally non-sinusoidal but periodic. It can be expanded into a Fourier series. If the linear part of the system acts as a severe low-pass filter, the higher harmonics (\( 2\omega, 3\omega, \dots \)) are highly attenuated. Harmonic linearization approximates the nonlinearity by considering only the fundamental harmonic component of the output.

Describing Function Block Diagram Replacement

Mathematical Definition:

Let the input to the nonlinear element be \( x(t) = X \sin(\omega t) \).

The describing function is \( K_N(X) = \frac{A_1}{X} \angle 0^\circ \):

$$ K_N(X) = \begin{cases} K & \text{for } X \le S \\ \frac{2K}{\pi} \left[ \alpha + \sin\alpha \cos\alpha \right] & \text{for } X > S \end{cases} $$
$$ \text{Where } \alpha = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{S}{X}\right) $$

4.3 Dead-Zone Nonlinearity

Output is zero for inputs between \(-D/2\) and \(D/2\). Let threshold be \( S = D/2 \). Let input be \( x(t) = X \sin(\omega t) \) where \( X > S \). Let \( x(t) = S \) at \( \omega t = \alpha \). Therefore, \( \alpha = \sin^{-1}(S/X) \).

$$ y(\omega t) = \begin{cases} 0, & 0 \le \omega t \le \alpha \\ K(X \sin\omega t - S), & \alpha \le \omega t \le \pi/2 \end{cases} $$

Because the output is an odd function, \( B_1 = 0 \). Using quarter-wave symmetry for \( A_1 \):

$$ A_1 = \frac{4}{\pi} \int_{\alpha}^{\pi/2} K(X \sin\omega t - S) \sin(\omega t) d(\omega t) $$ $$ A_1 = \frac{4K}{\pi} \left[ X \int_{\alpha}^{\pi/2} \sin^2(\omega t) d(\omega t) - S \int_{\alpha}^{\pi/2} \sin(\omega t) d(\omega t) \right] $$

Evaluate the integrals:

$$ A_1 = \frac{4K}{\pi} \left[ X \left( \frac{\omega t}{2} - \frac{\sin 2\omega t}{4} \right)_\alpha^{\pi/2} - S [-\cos\omega t]_\alpha^{\pi/2} \right] $$ $$ A_1 = \frac{4K}{\pi} \left[ X \left( \frac{\pi}{4} - \frac{\alpha}{2} + \frac{\sin 2\alpha}{4} \right) - S \cos\alpha \right] $$

Substitute \( S = X \sin\alpha \):

$$ A_1 = \frac{KX}{\pi} \left[ \pi - 2\alpha + \sin 2\alpha - 4 \sin\alpha \cos\alpha \right] $$ $$ A_1 = \frac{KX}{\pi} \left[ \pi - 2\alpha + 2\sin\alpha\cos\alpha - 4\sin\alpha\cos\alpha \right] $$ $$ A_1 = \frac{KX}{\pi} \left[ \pi - 2\alpha - 2\sin\alpha\cos\alpha \right] = \frac{2KX}{\pi} \left[ \frac{\pi}{2} - \alpha - \sin\alpha\cos\alpha \right] $$

The describing function is \( K_N(X) = \frac{A_1}{X} \angle 0^\circ \):

$$ K_N(X) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{for } X \le S \\ \frac{2K}{\pi} \left[ \frac{\pi}{2} - \alpha - \sin\alpha \cos\alpha \right] & \text{for } X > S \end{cases} $$
$$ \text{Where } \alpha = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{S}{X}\right) $$

5 Stability Analysis using Describing Functions

HIGHLY EXAMINED TENTH-MARKER Apr 2018May 2019

The Intersection Method

The characteristic equation of a nonlinear closed-loop system is given by:

$$ 1 + K_N(X, \omega) G(j\omega) = 0 \implies G(j\omega) = \frac{-1}{K_N(X, \omega)} $$

Stability analysis is performed by plotting both \( G(j\omega) \) (polar plot) and \( -1/K_N \) on the same complex plane.

  • No Intersection: If \( G(j\omega) \) does not enclose the \( -1/K_N \) locus, the system is stable.
  • Intersection: The points of intersection indicate the existence of Limit Cycles. At the intersection point:
    • The frequency of the limit cycle (\( \omega \)) is found from the \( G(j\omega) \) curve.
    • The amplitude of the limit cycle (\( X \)) is found from the \( -1/K_N \) curve.
SOLVED UNIVERSITY QP (APR 2018 / MAY 2019)

Comprehensive Stability Problem

Question: Consider a unity feedback system having a saturation amplifier with gain \( K \) in the linear region (saturation limit \( S=1 \)) in cascade with a linear plant \( G(s) = \frac{1}{s(1+0.5s)(1+4s)} \).
(a) Determine the maximum value of \( K \) for the system to remain stable.
(b) If \( K=2.5 \), determine the amplitude and frequency of the resulting limit cycle.

Part (a): Maximum K for Stability

The linear transfer function including gain \( K \) is:

$$ G(j\omega) = \frac{K}{j\omega(1 + j0.5\omega)(1 + j4\omega)} $$

The magnitude and phase are:

$$ |G(j\omega)| = \frac{K}{\omega\sqrt{1 + 0.25\omega^2}\sqrt{1 + 16\omega^2}} $$ $$ \angle G(j\omega) = -90^\circ - \tan^{-1}(0.5\omega) - \tan^{-1}(4\omega) $$

For saturation nonlinearity, the describing function \( K_N \) decreases from \( K \) to 0 as \( X \) increases. The locus of \( -1/K_N \) starts at \( -1/K \) and travels along the negative real axis towards \( -\infty \).

The limiting condition for stability is when the \( G(j\omega) \) locus crosses the real axis at the start of the \( -1/K_N \) locus, which is at \( -1 \angle -180^\circ \) (assuming \( K \) is lumped into \( G(j\omega) \) and \( K_N \) max is 1).

Find the phase crossover frequency \( \omega_{pc} \) where \( \angle G(j\omega) = -180^\circ \):

$$ -90^\circ - \tan^{-1}(0.5\omega_{pc}) - \tan^{-1}(4\omega_{pc}) = -180^\circ $$ $$ \tan^{-1}(0.5\omega_{pc}) + \tan^{-1}(4\omega_{pc}) = 90^\circ $$

Taking \(\tan\) on both sides:

$$ \frac{0.5\omega_{pc} + 4\omega_{pc}}{1 - (0.5\omega_{pc})(4\omega_{pc})} = \tan(90^\circ) = \infty $$ $$ \implies \text{Denominator} = 0 \implies 1 - 2\omega_{pc}^2 = 0 \implies \omega_{pc}^2 = 0.5 \implies \omega_{pc} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \text{ rad/sec} $$

At marginal stability, \( |G(j\omega_{pc})| = 1 \):

$$ \frac{K}{\omega_{pc}\sqrt{1 + 0.25\omega_{pc}^2}\sqrt{1 + 16\omega_{pc}^2}} = 1 $$ $$ K = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \sqrt{1 + 0.25(0.5)} \sqrt{1 + 16(0.5)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \sqrt{1.125} \sqrt{9} $$ $$ K = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \times 1.0606 \times 3 = 2.25 $$

Therefore, the maximum value of K for the system to be stable is \( K < 2.25 \).

Part (b): Limit Cycle for \( K=2.5 \)

If \( K = 2.5 \), the system is unstable under linear conditions. The \( G(j\omega) \) locus will intersect the \( -1/K_N \) locus, predicting a stable limit cycle.

The frequency of the limit cycle is dictated by the point where the locus crosses the negative real axis. As derived above, this crossing always occurs at:

$$ \omega = \omega_{pc} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 0.707 \text{ rad/sec} $$

The amplitude is determined by the intersection point condition:

$$ |G(j\omega_{pc})|_{\text{with } K=2.5} = \frac{2.5}{2.25} = 1.111 $$

At intersection, \( |G(j\omega)| = \left|\frac{-1}{K_N(X)}\right| \implies K_N(X) = \frac{1}{1.111} = 0.9 \).

Since \( K_N(X) < 1 \), the system operates in the saturated region (\( X > S=1 \)). Using the describing function for saturation:

$$ \frac{2}{\pi} \left[ \alpha + \sin\alpha \cos\alpha \right] = 0.9 \quad \text{where } \alpha = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{X}\right) $$

Solving this transcendental equation (usually done graphically or numerically) yields the amplitude \( X \).

Result: A stable limit cycle exists with frequency \(\omega \approx 0.707\) rad/sec.
