Lyapunov's Second Method of Stability

Advanced Control Theory for Nonlinear Systems

The second method is Lyapunov's second or direct method: this is a generalization of Lagrange's concept of stability of minimum potential energy.

Consider a nonlinear system. Without loss of generality, we assume the origin as the equilibrium point of the system. Let us define the following:

Lyapunov Function Candidate

A scalar function $V(x)$ will be a Lyapunov function candidate if it has the following properties:

  • 1 $V(0) = 0$ for $x = 0$
  • 2 $V(x) > 0$ for $x \neq 0$

*Note: A function satisfying these conditions is mathematically referred to as being Positive Definite.

The "Energy" Analogy

Think of a marble rolling inside a bowl. We intuitively know the marble will eventually settle at the bottom (the equilibrium point) because friction causes it to lose mechanical energy over time.

Lyapunov generalized this physical concept. He proposed that if a system has an equilibrium point, and we can find a scalar "energy-like" function for that system, we can determine its stability by observing how that function behaves over time. If the "energy" of the system is strictly decreasing along all trajectories, the system will eventually come to rest at the equilibrium point.

Furthermore, if $V(x)$ is such that

$$\frac{dV(x)}{dt} < 0$$

, then it is called a Lyapunov function. The origin of the system will be asymptotically stable if there exists a Lyapunov function along the trajectories of the system.

Calculating the Derivative along Trajectories

To prove stability, the time derivative of $V(x)$ must be Negative Definite. This is calculated using the chain rule and substituting the system's state equations ($\dot{x} = f(x)$):

$$\dot{V}(x) = \frac{\partial V}{\partial x_1}\dot{x_1} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial x_2}\dot{x_2} + \dots + \frac{\partial V}{\partial x_n}\dot{x_n} = \nabla V \cdot f(x)$$

The Catch: If you find a $V(x)$ that satisfies these conditions, the system is stable. However, if you cannot find such a function, it does not mean the system is unstable. It simply means your chosen $V(x)$ wasn't the correct candidate.

Solved Example

Problem:

Use Lyapunov direct method to check the stability of the system described by the state model given below. Comment on the global stability of the system.

$$\dot{X} = \begin{bmatrix} -2 & 5 \\ 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix} X$$

Solution:

This is a linear time-invariant (LTI) system of the form $\dot{X} = AX$. For LTI systems, we use the continuous-time Lyapunov equation:

$$A^T P + PA = -Q$$

Step 1: Define the system matrix $A$ and choose a positive definite matrix $Q$ (typically the Identity matrix $I$).

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} -2 & 5 \\ 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix}, \quad Q = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 2: Let $P$ be a symmetric matrix $P = \begin{bmatrix} p_{11} & p_{12} \\ p_{12} & p_{22} \end{bmatrix}$. Substitute into the Lyapunov equation:

$$\begin{bmatrix} -2 & 0 \\ 5 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} p_{11} & p_{12} \\ p_{12} & p_{22} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} p_{11} & p_{12} \\ p_{12} & p_{22} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} -2 & 5 \\ 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 3: Multiply and add the matrices on the Left Hand Side (LHS):

$$\begin{bmatrix} -4p_{11} & 5p_{11}-4p_{12} \\ 5p_{11}-4p_{12} & 10p_{12}-4p_{22} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 4: Equate corresponding elements to solve for $p_{11}, p_{12}, \text{ and } p_{22}$:

  • From $(1,1)$: $-4p_{11} = -1 \implies \mathbf{p_{11} = 1/4}$
  • From $(1,2)$: $5p_{11} - 4p_{12} = 0 \implies 5(1/4) = 4p_{12} \implies \mathbf{p_{12} = 5/16}$
  • From $(2,2)$: $10p_{12} - 4p_{22} = -1 \implies 10(5/16) + 1 = 4p_{22} \implies \frac{50}{16} + \frac{16}{16} = 4p_{22} \implies \mathbf{p_{22} = 33/32}$
$$P = \begin{bmatrix} 1/4 & 5/16 \\ 5/16 & 33/32 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 5: Check Positive Definiteness of P (Sylvester's Criterion)

  • First principal minor: $\Delta_1 = p_{11} = 1/4 > 0$
  • Second principal minor (Determinant): $\Delta_2 = |P| = (1/4)(33/32) - (5/16)^2 = \frac{33}{128} - \frac{25}{256} = \frac{66-25}{256} = \frac{41}{256} > 0$

Since both principal minors are positive, matrix $P$ is positive definite.

Therefore, the origin of the system is Asymptotically Stable.

Comment on Global Stability:

For linear systems ($\dot{X} = AX$), if the origin is asymptotically stable, it is inherently Globally Asymptotically Stable. The system will return to the origin from any initial state in the entire state space.

Solved Example 2: Finding Equilibrium Points

Problem:

Identify the equilibrium points for the system:

$$\begin{align*} \dot{x}_1 &= x_2 \\ \dot{x}_2 &= -0.5x_2 - \sin(x_1) \end{align*}$$

Solution:

By definition, a system is at an equilibrium point when its states are not changing over time. Therefore, we must set the state derivatives to zero:

$$\dot{x}_1 = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \dot{x}_2 = 0$$

Step 1: From the first equation, setting $\dot{x}_1 = 0$ immediately gives us:

$$x_2 = 0$$

Step 2: Substitute $x_2 = 0$ into the second equation and set it to zero:

$$-0.5(0) - \sin(x_1) = 0 \implies \sin(x_1) = 0$$

Step 3: Solve the trigonometric equation for $x_1$. The sine function is zero at integer multiples of $\pi$:

$$x_1 = n\pi, \quad \text{where } n = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \dots$$

The equilibrium points for the system are:

$$(n\pi, 0) \quad \text{for } n \in \mathbb{Z}$$

Physical Insight:

This system represents the equations of a damped pendulum. The state $x_1$ is the angle, and $x_2$ is the angular velocity. The equilibrium points $(0,0), (\pm 2\pi, 0), \dots$ represent the pendulum resting straight down (stable), while $(\pm \pi, 0), (\pm 3\pi, 0), \dots$ represent it balanced perfectly upright (unstable).

Solved Example 3: Nonlinear System Stability

Problem:

Consider a non linear system described by the following equations. Investigate the stability using Lyapunov's method.

$$\begin{align*} \dot{x}_1 &= x_2 \\ \dot{x}_2 &= -x_1^3 - x_2 \end{align*}$$

Hint: Candidate $V(x) = x_1^4 + x_1^2 + 2x_1x_2 + 2x_2^2$

Solution:

Step 1: Verify $V(x)$ is Positive Definite

We must ensure $V(x) > 0$ for all $x \neq 0$. Let's rearrange the terms in the candidate function by splitting $2x_2^2$ into $x_2^2 + x_2^2$ to complete a square:

$$\begin{align*} V(x) &= x_1^4 + x_1^2 + 2x_1x_2 + x_2^2 + x_2^2 \\ &= x_1^4 + (x_1^2 + 2x_1x_2 + x_2^2) + x_2^2 \\ &= x_1^4 + (x_1 + x_2)^2 + x_2^2 \end{align*}$$

Since $x_1^4 \geq 0$, $(x_1 + x_2)^2 \geq 0$, and $x_2^2 \geq 0$, their sum is strictly greater than zero for any state other than the origin. Thus, $V(x)$ is Positive Definite.

Step 2: Calculate the time derivative $\dot{V}(x)$

Using the chain rule: $\dot{V}(x) = \frac{\partial V}{\partial x_1}\dot{x_1} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial x_2}\dot{x_2}$. First, find the partial derivatives:

$$\frac{\partial V}{\partial x_1} = 4x_1^3 + 2x_1 + 2x_2$$ $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial x_2} = 2x_1 + 4x_2$$

Step 3: Substitute the system equations

Substitute $\dot{x}_1 = x_2$ and $\dot{x}_2 = -x_1^3 - x_2$ into the $\dot{V}(x)$ equation:

$$\dot{V}(x) = (4x_1^3 + 2x_1 + 2x_2)(x_2) + (2x_1 + 4x_2)(-x_1^3 - x_2)$$

Step 4: Expand and Simplify

$$\begin{align*} \dot{V}(x) &= [4x_1^3x_2 + 2x_1x_2 + 2x_2^2] + [-2x_1^4 - 2x_1x_2 - 4x_1^3x_2 - 4x_2^2] \\ &= (4x_1^3x_2 - 4x_1^3x_2) + (2x_1x_2 - 2x_1x_2) + (2x_2^2 - 4x_2^2) - 2x_1^4 \\ &= -2x_2^2 - 2x_1^4 \\ &= -2(x_1^4 + x_2^2) \end{align*}$$

We obtained $\dot{V}(x) = -2(x_1^4 + x_2^2)$.

Since $x_1^4$ and $x_2^2$ are always positive (for $x \neq 0$), the entire expression is strictly negative. Therefore, $\dot{V}(x)$ is Negative Definite.

Conclusion: The origin of the system is Asymptotically Stable.

Solved Example 4: Testing Two Systems

Problem:

Test the stability of the system using Lyapunov stability theorem

$(a) \quad \dot{x}_1 = -x_1 + 2x_1^2x_2, \quad \dot{x}_2 = -x_2$

$(b) \quad \dot{x}_1 = x_2, \quad \dot{x}_2 = -\sin(x_1) - x_2$

Solution for System (a):

Step 1: Choose a Lyapunov Candidate

Let's select a standard quadratic function which is clearly positive definite:

$$V(x) = \frac{1}{2}x_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}x_2^2$$

Step 2: Calculate $\dot{V}(x)$

$$\begin{align*} \dot{V}(x) &= \frac{\partial V}{\partial x_1}\dot{x}_1 + \frac{\partial V}{\partial x_2}\dot{x}_2 \\ &= x_1(-x_1 + 2x_1^2x_2) + x_2(-x_2) \\ &= -x_1^2 + 2x_1^3x_2 - x_2^2 \end{align*}$$

Step 3: Analyze Definiteness

Let's rewrite the derivative to understand its behavior near the origin:

$$\dot{V}(x) = -(x_1^2 + x_2^2) + 2x_1^3x_2$$

Near the origin (when $x_1$ and $x_2$ are very small), the quadratic term $-(x_1^2 + x_2^2)$ strongly dominates the higher-order fourth-degree term $2x_1^3x_2$. Therefore, in a sufficiently small neighborhood around the origin, $\dot{V}(x) < 0$.

Conclusion for (a): The origin is Locally Asymptotically Stable.

Solution for System (b):

This represents a pendulum with friction. We use the system's physical total energy (Potential + Kinetic) as our candidate.

Step 1: Choose a Lyapunov Candidate

$$V(x) = (1 - \cos x_1) + \frac{1}{2}x_2^2$$

Note: $V(x) > 0$ for $x \neq 0$ locally (within $-\pi < x_1 < \pi$), so it is Positive Definite.

Step 2: Calculate $\dot{V}(x)$

$$\begin{align*} \dot{V}(x) &= (\sin x_1)\dot{x}_1 + x_2\dot{x}_2 \\ &= (\sin x_1)(x_2) + x_2(-\sin x_1 - x_2) \\ &= x_2\sin x_1 - x_2\sin x_1 - x_2^2 \\ &= -x_2^2 \end{align*}$$

Step 3: Analyze Definiteness (LaSalle's Principle)

Here, $\dot{V}(x) = -x_2^2 \leq 0$. This makes it Negative Semi-Definite (it can be zero even if $x_1 \neq 0$). This guarantees the system is at least Stable.

Applying LaSalle's Invariance Principle: To prove asymptotic stability, we check the set where $\dot{V}(x) = 0$. This occurs when $x_2 = 0$. If $x_2 = 0$, then $\dot{x}_2 = 0$. Substituting this into the system's second equation: $0 = -\sin(x_1) - 0 \implies \sin(x_1) = 0 \implies x_1 = 0$ (in the local region). Since the only invariant point is the origin itself, the system is forced to settle there.

Conclusion for (b): The origin is Asymptotically Stable.

Conclusion

We can see that the method hinges on the existence of a Lyapunov function, which is an energy-like function, zero at equilibrium, positive definite everywhere else, and continuously decreasing as we approach the equilibrium.
