1 Controllability & Observability

DEFINITIONS & KALMAN'S TEST Apr 2018May 2019Dec 2019

State Controllability

A system is said to be completely state controllable if it is possible to transfer the system state from any initial state $X(t_0)$ to any other desired state $X(t_f)$ in a specified finite time by applying a finite unconstrained control vector $U(t)$.

Kalman's Controllability Test

Construct the composite controllability matrix $Q_c$:

$$ Q_c = \begin{bmatrix} B & AB & A^2B & \dots & A^{n-1}B \end{bmatrix} $$

Condition: The system is completely controllable if the rank of $Q_c$ is $n$ (i.e., $|Q_c| \neq 0$).

State Observability

A system is said to be completely observable if every state $X(t_0)$ can be uniquely determined from the observation of the output $Y(t)$ over a finite time interval $t_0 \le t \le t_f$, given the control input $U(t)$.

Kalman's Observability Test

Construct the composite observability matrix $Q_o$:

$$ Q_o = \begin{bmatrix} C^T & A^TC^T & (A^T)^2C^T & \dots & (A^T)^{n-1}C^T \end{bmatrix} $$

Condition: The system is completely observable if the rank of $Q_o$ is $n$ (i.e., $|Q_o| \neq 0$).

SOLVED QP (MAY 2019)

Problem: Checking Controllability from Transfer Function

Question: Define controllability and observability of a system and check whether the system $\frac{Y(s)}{U(s)} = \frac{1}{(s+1)(s+2)}$ is controllable or not.

Step 1: Convert Transfer Function to Differential Equation

$$ \frac{Y(s)}{U(s)} = \frac{1}{s^2 + 3s + 2} $$ $$ Y(s)[s^2 + 3s + 2] = U(s) $$

Taking the inverse Laplace transform:

$$ \ddot{y} + 3\dot{y} + 2y = u $$

Step 2: Define State Variables

Let $x_1 = y$ and $x_2 = \dot{y}$.

1) $\dot{x}_1 = x_2$

2) $\dot{x}_2 = \ddot{y} = -2y - 3\dot{y} + u = -2x_1 - 3x_2 + u$

State Space Model (Phase Variable Form):

$$ \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x}_1 \\ \dot{x}_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -2 & -3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} u $$

Step 3: Kalman's Controllability Test

Identify matrices $A$ and $B$:

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -2 & -3 \end{bmatrix}, \quad B = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Calculate $AB$:

$$ AB = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -2 & -3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} $$

Form the controllability matrix $Q_c = [B \quad AB]$:

$$ Q_c = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & -3 \end{bmatrix} $$

Calculate the determinant:

$$ |Q_c| = (0)(-3) - (1)(1) = -1 \neq 0 $$

Since $|Q_c| \neq 0$, the rank of $Q_c$ is 2 ($=n$). Therefore, the system is completely state controllable.

SOLVED QP

Problem: Parametric Controllability

Question: Consider the system given below:
$\dot{X} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -3 \end{bmatrix} x + \begin{bmatrix} 1 & a \\ 0 & b \end{bmatrix} U$.
Find the range of values of "a" and "b" for the system to be controllable.

Step 1: Calculate the Controllability Matrix $Q_c$

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -3 \end{bmatrix}, \quad B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & a \\ 0 & b \end{bmatrix} $$

Calculate $AB$:

$$ AB = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & a \\ 0 & b \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & -a \\ 0 & -3b \end{bmatrix} $$

Form $Q_c = [B \quad AB]$:

$$ Q_c = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & a & -1 & -a \\ 0 & b & 0 & -3b \end{bmatrix} $$

Step 2: Determine Rank Condition

For the system to be completely controllable, the rank of the $2 \times 4$ matrix $Q_c$ must be 2. This requires at least one $2 \times 2$ minor to have a non-zero determinant.

Let's check the determinants of the $2 \times 2$ submatrices:

  • Cols 1 & 2: $\begin{vmatrix} 1 & a \\ 0 & b \end{vmatrix} = b$
  • Cols 1 & 4: $\begin{vmatrix} 1 & -a \\ 0 & -3b \end{vmatrix} = -3b$
  • Cols 2 & 4: $\begin{vmatrix} a & -a \\ b & -3b \end{vmatrix} = -3ab - (-ab) = -2ab$

If $b = 0$, the entire second row of $Q_c$ becomes zeros, and the rank becomes 1. As long as $b \neq 0$, there is a non-zero minor.

Range of values: $a \in \mathbb{R}$ (any real value), and $b \neq 0$.

3 Sampled Data Control Systems

CONCEPTS & DERIVATIONS May 2019Sep 2020

A sampled-data control system incorporates both continuous-time (analog) and discrete-time (digital) signals. A sampler converts analog signals into discrete pulses, and a hold circuit (usually Zero-Order Hold) reconstructs an analog continuous staircase signal from the digital pulses to drive the plant.

Block Schematic of a System Controlled by a Digital Controller

Additional Components in a Digital Control System:
  • Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC): Samples the continuous-time error signal $e(t)$ at discrete intervals and converts it into a digital/binary format $e(k)$ that the computer can process. It incorporates the sampling (sampler) function.
  • Digital Controller (Microprocessor/Computer): Processes the digital error signal according to a programmed control algorithm (like discrete PID) to generate a digital control signal $u(k)$.
  • Digital-to-Analog Converter (DAC): Converts the digital control sequence $u(k)$ back into a continuous-time analog signal $u(t)$ to drive the physical plant. It usually holds the signal constant between sampling intervals (acting as a Zero-Order Hold).
  • Clock (Timer): Provides synchronization pulses to ensure the ADC, Digital Controller, and DAC operate at precisely timed sampling intervals $T$.
THEORY QUESTION

Structure of Digital State Space Representation

Question: Write the structure of state space representation of an $n^{th}$ ordered SISO system in digital domain and specify the dimensions of each matrix.

For a discrete-time (digital) Single-Input Single-Output (SISO) system, the state space model is written as difference equations:

$$ X(k+1) = G X(k) + H u(k) \quad \text{(State Equation)} $$ $$ y(k) = C X(k) + D u(k) \quad \text{(Output Equation)} $$

Where the dimensions for an $n^{th}$ order SISO system are:

  • $X(k)$: State vector of dimension $n \times 1$
  • $u(k)$: Control input of dimension $1 \times 1$ (scalar)
  • $y(k)$: Output of dimension $1 \times 1$ (scalar)
  • $G$: Discrete System matrix of dimension $n \times n$
  • $H$: Discrete Input matrix of dimension $n \times 1$
  • $C$: Discrete Output matrix of dimension $1 \times n$
  • $D$: Feedforward gain of dimension $1 \times 1$ (scalar)

3.1 Pulse Transfer Function & Zero Order Hold (ZOH)

What is Pulse Transfer Function?

The transfer function of a Linear Discrete System (LDS) is given by the z-transform of its impulse response. The transfer function of LDS is also called the z-transfer function or pulse transfer function.

Let $h(k) =$ Impulse response of a LDS system.

Now, the z-transform of $h(k) = H(z)$.

The transfer function of the LDS system or Pulse transfer function is given by:

$$H(z) = \frac{C(z)}{R(z)}$$

Alternatively, the pulse transfer function relates the z-transform of the output at the sampling instants to the z-transform of the sampled input. When the same system is subject to a sampled data or digital signal $r^*(t)$, the output of the system is $C(s) = G(s)R^*(s)$.

The transfer function of the above system is difficult to manipulate because it contains a mixture of analog and digital components. Thus, it is desirable to express the system characteristics by a transfer function that relates $r^*(t)$ to $c^*(t)$, a fictitious sampler output. Then,

$$G^*(s) = \frac{C^*(s)}{R^*(s)}$$

is known as the pulse transfer function. Sometimes it is also referred to as the starred transfer function. If we now substitute $z = e^{Ts}$ in the previous expression, we will directly get the Z-transfer function $G(z)$ as $G(z) = \frac{C(z)}{R(z)}$.

Derivation of ZOH Transfer Function

The ZOH reconstructs the signal by holding the sampled value constant until the next sampling instant. Its impulse response $h(t)$ is a rectangular pulse of unit height and width $T$ (the sampling period).

Mathematically, the impulse response is:

$$ h(t) = u(t) - u(t - T) $$

Taking the Laplace transform gives the Transfer Function of ZOH ($G_{ZOH}(s)$):

$$ G_{ZOH}(s) = \mathcal{L}[u(t)] - \mathcal{L}[u(t - T)] = \frac{1}{s} - \frac{e^{-sT}}{s} = \frac{1 - e^{-sT}}{s} $$
SOLVED QP (MAY 2019)

Problem: Finding Pulse Transfer Function

Question: Determine the pulse transfer function of a discrete-time control system having a ZOH cascaded with a plant $G(s) = \frac{1}{s+1}$. Assume sampling time $T = 1$ sec. Also, find the response to a unit step input.

Step 1: Formulate the Overall Z-Transform

The overall pulse transfer function $G(z)$ is the Z-transform of the cascaded ZOH and plant:

$$ G(z) = \mathcal{Z}\{G_{ZOH}(s) G(s)\} = \mathcal{Z}\left\{ \frac{1 - e^{-sT}}{s} \cdot \frac{1}{s+1} \right\} $$

Since $(1 - e^{-sT})$ represents a delay of 1 sample, its Z-transform equivalent is $(1 - z^{-1})$. We can pull it out of the Z-transform operator:

$$ G(z) = (1 - z^{-1}) \mathcal{Z}\left\{ \frac{1}{s(s+1)} \right\} = \frac{z-1}{z} \mathcal{Z}\left\{ \frac{1}{s(s+1)} \right\} $$

Step 2: Partial Fractions and Z-Transform

Expand the term inside the Z-transform using partial fractions:

$$ \frac{1}{s(s+1)} = \frac{A}{s} + \frac{B}{s+1} $$

Solving gives $A = 1, B = -1$:

$$ \frac{1}{s(s+1)} = \frac{1}{s} - \frac{1}{s+1} $$

Now take the standard Z-transforms ($\mathcal{Z}\{\frac{1}{s}\} = \frac{z}{z-1}$ and $\mathcal{Z}\{\frac{1}{s+a}\} = \frac{z}{z-e^{-aT}}$):

$$ \mathcal{Z}\left\{ \frac{1}{s} - \frac{1}{s+1} \right\} = \frac{z}{z-1} - \frac{z}{z - e^{-T}} $$

Substitute $T = 1$ sec, $e^{-1} \approx 0.368$:

$$ \dots = \frac{z}{z-1} - \frac{z}{z-0.368} = \frac{z(z-0.368) - z(z-1)}{(z-1)(z-0.368)} = \frac{z^2 - 0.368z - z^2 + z}{(z-1)(z-0.368)} = \frac{0.632z}{(z-1)(z-0.368)} $$

Step 3: Final Pulse Transfer Function

Multiply back the $(1 - z^{-1}) = \frac{z-1}{z}$ term:

$$ G(z) = \left( \frac{z-1}{z} \right) \left[ \frac{0.632z}{(z-1)(z-0.368)} \right] = \frac{0.632}{z - 0.368} $$

Step 4: Response to Unit Step Input

For a unit step input, $r(t) = u(t)$, the Z-transform is $R(z) = \frac{z}{z-1}$.

Output $C(z) = G(z) R(z)$:

$$ C(z) = \left( \frac{0.632}{z - 0.368} \right) \left( \frac{z}{z-1} \right) $$

Divide by $z$ to do partial fractions: $\frac{C(z)}{z} = \frac{0.632}{(z-1)(z-0.368)} = \frac{A}{z-1} + \frac{B}{z-0.368}$.

  • $A = \left. \frac{0.632}{z - 0.368} \right|_{z=1} = \frac{0.632}{1 - 0.368} = \frac{0.632}{0.632} = 1$
  • $B = \left. \frac{0.632}{z - 1} \right|_{z=0.368} = \frac{0.632}{0.368 - 1} = \frac{0.632}{-0.632} = -1$
$$ \frac{C(z)}{z} = \frac{1}{z-1} - \frac{1}{z-0.368} \implies C(z) = \frac{z}{z-1} - \frac{z}{z-0.368} $$

Taking inverse Z-transform ($Z^{-1}\{\frac{z}{z-a}\} = a^k$):

$$ c(kT) = 1^k - (0.368)^k = 1 - (0.368)^k \quad \text{for } k \ge 0 $$
SOLVED QP

Problem: Pulse Transfer Function from Difference Eq

Question: A sample data system is described by the following equation:
$y(k) + 3y(k-1) + 2y(k-2) = u(k) + 5u(k-1)$.
Obtain the pulse transfer function.

Step 1: Take the Z-Transform

Take the Z-transform on both sides, assuming zero initial conditions. Recall that the delay property states $\mathcal{Z}\{y(k-n)\} = z^{-n}Y(z)$.

$$ Y(z) + 3z^{-1}Y(z) + 2z^{-2}Y(z) = U(z) + 5z^{-1}U(z) $$

Step 2: Factor and Isolate Transfer Function

Factor out $Y(z)$ and $U(z)$:

$$ Y(z) [1 + 3z^{-1} + 2z^{-2}] = U(z) [1 + 5z^{-1}] $$

The pulse transfer function is $H(z) = \frac{Y(z)}{U(z)}$:

$$ H(z) = \frac{1 + 5z^{-1}}{1 + 3z^{-1} + 2z^{-2}} $$

Step 3: Convert to Positive Powers of z

Multiply the numerator and denominator by $z^2$ to eliminate negative powers:

$$ H(z) = \frac{z^2 + 5z}{z^2 + 3z + 2} = \frac{z(z+5)}{(z+1)(z+2)} $$

Conclusion: All necessary and sufficient conditions are satisfied. Hence, the system is STABLE.

SOLVED QP

Problem: Stability of $z^4 + 0.6z^3 + 0.63z^2 - 0.37z + 0.065 = 0$

Question: Determine the stability of the system with characteristic equation $z^4 + 0.6z^3 + 0.63z^2 - 0.37z + 0.065 = 0$.

Step 1: Identify Coefficients

  • $a_0 = 1$
  • $a_1 = 0.6$
  • $a_2 = 0.63$
  • $a_3 = -0.37$
  • $a_4 = 0.065$

Step 2: Check Necessary Conditions

  • 1. $P(1) > 0$:
    $P(1) = 1 + 0.6 + 0.63 - 0.37 + 0.065 = 1.925$. Since $1.925 > 0$, condition satisfied.
  • 2. $(-1)^n P(-1) > 0$ (here $n=4$):
    $(-1)^4 P(-1) = 1(1 - 0.6 + 0.63 + 0.37 + 0.065) = 1.465$. Since $1.465 > 0$, condition satisfied.

Step 3: Construct Jury Array

Calculation for Row 3 elements ($b_k$):

$$ b_3 = \begin{vmatrix} a_4 & a_3 \\ a_0 & a_1 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} 0.065 & -0.37 \\ 1 & 0.6 \end{vmatrix} = 0.065(0.6) - (-0.37) = 0.039 + 0.37 = 0.409 $$
$$ b_2 = \begin{vmatrix} a_4 & a_2 \\ a_0 & a_2 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} 0.065 & 0.63 \\ 1 & 0.63 \end{vmatrix} = 0.065(0.63) - 0.63 = 0.04095 - 0.63 = -0.58905 $$
$$ b_1 = \begin{vmatrix} a_4 & a_1 \\ a_0 & a_3 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} 0.065 & 0.6 \\ 1 & -0.37 \end{vmatrix} = 0.065(-0.37) - 0.6 = -0.02405 - 0.6 = -0.62405 $$
$$ b_0 = \begin{vmatrix} a_4 & a_0 \\ a_0 & a_4 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} 0.065 & 1 \\ 1 & 0.065 \end{vmatrix} = 0.065^2 - 1 = 0.004225 - 1 = -0.995775 $$

Calculation for Row 5 elements ($c_k$):

$$ c_2 = \begin{vmatrix} b_0 & b_1 \\ b_3 & b_2 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} -0.995775 & -0.62405 \\ 0.409 & -0.58905 \end{vmatrix} = 0.58656 - (-0.25523) = 0.8418 $$
$$ c_1 = \begin{vmatrix} b_0 & b_2 \\ b_3 & b_1 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} -0.995775 & -0.58905 \\ 0.409 & -0.62405 \end{vmatrix} = 0.62141 - (-0.24092) = 0.8623 $$
$$ c_0 = \begin{vmatrix} b_0 & b_3 \\ b_3 & b_0 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} -0.995775 & 0.409 \\ 0.409 & -0.995775 \end{vmatrix} = 0.99156 - 0.16728 = 0.8243 $$

Step 4: Check Sufficient Conditions

  • 1. $|a_4| < |a_0|$: $0.065 < 1$ (Satisfied).
  • 2. $|b_0| > |b_3|$: $|-0.9957| > |0.409|$ (Satisfied).
  • 3. $|c_0| > |c_2|$: $|0.8243| > |0.8418|$ FALSE!

Conclusion: Since $|c_0| \ngtr |c_2|$, the sufficient conditions are violated. Hence, the system is UNSTABLE.

SOLVED QP

Problem: Stability of $2z^4 + 7z^3 + 10z^2 + 4z + 1 = 0$

Question: Consider the system with characteristic polynomial $F(z) = 2z^4 + 7z^3 + 10z^2 + 4z + 1$. Investigate the stability of the system using Jury's Test.

Step 1: Check Necessary Conditions

$a_0 = 2, a_1 = 7, a_2 = 10, a_3 = 4, a_4 = 1$.

  • 1. $F(1) > 0$:
    $F(1) = 2 + 7 + 10 + 4 + 1 = 24$. Since $24 > 0$, condition satisfied.
  • 2. $(-1)^4 F(-1) > 0$:
    $(-1)^4 F(-1) = 1(2 - 7 + 10 - 4 + 1) = 2$. Since $2 > 0$, condition satisfied.

Step 2: Construct Jury Array

Calculation for Row 3 elements ($b_k$):

$$ b_0 = \begin{vmatrix} a_4 & a_0 \\ a_0 & a_4 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = (1)(1) - (2)(2) = 1 - 4 = -3 $$
$$ b_1 = \begin{vmatrix} a_4 & a_1 \\ a_0 & a_3 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} 1 & 7 \\ 2 & 4 \end{vmatrix} = (1)(4) - (7)(2) = 4 - 14 = -10 $$
$$ b_2 = \begin{vmatrix} a_4 & a_2 \\ a_0 & a_2 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} 1 & 10 \\ 2 & 10 \end{vmatrix} = (1)(10) - (10)(2) = 10 - 20 = -10 $$
$$ b_3 = \begin{vmatrix} a_4 & a_3 \\ a_0 & a_1 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ 2 & 7 \end{vmatrix} = (1)(7) - (4)(2) = 7 - 8 = -1 $$

Step 3: Check Sufficient Conditions

We check the magnitude constraints immediately to save time:

  • 1. $|a_4| < |a_0|$: $|1| < |2|$ (Satisfied).
  • 2. $|b_0| > |b_3|$: $|-3| > |-1| \implies 3 > 1$ FALSE!

Conclusion: Since $|b_0| \ngtr |b_3|$, the sufficient condition is violated early. Hence, the system is UNSTABLE.
