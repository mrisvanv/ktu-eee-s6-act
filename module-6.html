1 Introduction to Phase Plane Analysis

CONCEPTS May 2019Dec 2019

The phase plane method is a graphical technique used for investigating second-order nonlinear and linear differential equations. It provides a visual representation of the system dynamics without explicitly solving the differential equations analytically over time.

1. State and State Variables

State: The state of a dynamic system is the smallest set of variables such that the knowledge of these variables at time \( t=t_0 \) (initial condition), together with the input for \( t \ge t_0 \), completely determines the behavior of the system for any time \( t \ge t_0 \).

State Vector: If \( n \) state variables are needed to describe a system, they form an \( n \)-dimensional vector \( \mathbf{X} = [x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n]^T \).

2. State Space and Phase Plane

State Space: The \( n \)-dimensional space whose coordinate axes consist of the \( x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n \) axes is called a state space.

Phase Plane: For a second-order system (\( n=2 \)), the state space reduces to a two-dimensional plane called the Phase Plane, typically with \( x_1 \) and \( x_2 \) as coordinate axes.

3. Phase Trajectory

A curve in the state space (or phase plane) that shows the variation in the state of the system as time progresses is called a state trajectory or phase trajectory.

4. Phase Portrait

A family of phase trajectories corresponding to different initial conditions is called a phase portrait. It reveals the global qualitative behavior of the system (e.g., regions of stability, limit cycles).

2 Singular Points (Equilibrium Points)

VERY IMPORTANT Apr 2018May 2019Jul 2021

Definition: The points in the phase space at which the derivatives of all the state variables of an autonomous system are zero (i.e., \( \dot{x}_1 = 0, \dot{x}_2 = 0, \dots \)) are called singular points or equilibrium points. If the system is placed at such a point, it will continue to lie there if left undisturbed.

Depending on the roots of the system's characteristic equation (eigenvalues of the system matrix \( A \)), singular points are classified into distinct types. Let the characteristic equation be \( s^2 + a_1s + a_2 = 0 \).

1. Stable Node

Eigenvalues: Real, distinct, and both negative (\( \lambda_1, \lambda_2 < 0 \)).

2. Unstable Node

Eigenvalues: Real, distinct, and both positive (\( \lambda_1, \lambda_2 > 0 \)).

3. Saddle Point

Eigenvalues: Real, distinct, with opposite signs (\( \lambda_1 < 0, \lambda_2 > 0 \)). Unstable.

4. Stable Focus

Eigenvalues: Complex conjugates with negative real part (\( -\sigma \pm j\omega_d \)).

5. Unstable Focus

Eigenvalues: Complex conjugates with positive real part (\( +\sigma \pm j\omega_d \)).

6. Center or Vortex Point

Eigenvalues: Purely imaginary and conjugate (\( \pm j\omega_d \)). Exhibits sustained oscillations.

3 Limit Cycles

CONCEPTS & TYPES Apr 2018Dec 2019Jul 2021

The response of nonlinear systems may exhibit oscillations with fixed amplitude and frequency; these oscillations are called limit cycles. Once the system trajectories converge to a limit cycle, the system will continue to remain in this closed trajectory in the state space.

Unlike a "Center" in linear systems whose amplitude depends strictly on initial conditions, the amplitude and frequency of a limit cycle are inherent properties of the nonlinear system itself.

Stable Limit Cycle

All trajectories in the vicinity (both inside and outside) converge to the limit cycle as \( t \to \infty \).

Unstable Limit Cycle

All trajectories in the vicinity diverge away from the limit cycle as \( t \to \infty \).

Semi-Stable Limit Cycle

Trajectories converge from one side (e.g., inside) and diverge from the other side (e.g., outside).

4 Lyapunov Stability Analysis

ESSAY QUESTION (10 MARKS) Apr 2018May 2019Dec 2019Sep 2020Jul 2021

In general, a relaxed system (no disturbance and zero input) operates at an equilibrium point. A nonlinear system may have multiple equilibrium points. A Russian mathematician, A.M. Lyapunov, introduced two methods for stability analysis of nonlinear systems: the Indirect Method (First Method) and the Direct Method (Second Method).

4.1 Definition of Stability

Let \( x=0 \) be an equilibrium point of an autonomous system \( \dot{X} = F(X) \).

  • Stable (in the sense of Lyapunov): If, for each \( \epsilon > 0 \), there is \( \delta = \delta(\epsilon) > 0 \) such that if the initial state \( ||X(t_0)|| < \delta \), then the state trajectory \( ||X(t)|| < \epsilon \) for all \( t \ge t_0 \). (It stays within a bounded region).
  • Asymptotically Stable: If it is stable, and additionally, \( \delta \) can be chosen such that \( ||X(t_0)|| < \delta \) implies \( \lim_{t \to \infty} X(t) = 0 \). (It returns to the origin).
  • Unstable: If it is not stable.

4.2 Definiteness of Scalar Functions

Lyapunov's Direct Method requires constructing a scalar "energy-like" function, \( V(x) \). We must classify the sign definiteness of \( V(x) \):

1. Positive Definite

\( V(x) > 0 \) for all \( x \ne 0 \), and \( V(0) = 0 \).

Example: \( V(x) = 5x_1^2 + 2x_2^2 \)

2. Negative Definite

\( V(x) < 0 \) for all \( x \ne 0 \), and \( V(0) = 0 \).

Example: \( V(x) = -x_1^2 - 4x_2^2 \)

3. Positive Semi-Definite

\( V(x) \ge 0 \) for all \( x \), and \( V(0) = 0 \), but \( V(x) \) can be zero for some non-zero \( x \).

Example: \( V(x) = (2x_1 + 3x_2)^2 \) (Zero when \( 2x_1 = -3x_2 \))

4. Negative Semi-Definite

\( V(x) \le 0 \) for all \( x \), and \( V(0) = 0 \), but \( V(x) \) can be zero for some non-zero \( x \).

Example: \( V(x) = -(2x_1 + 3x_2)^2 \)

5. Indefinite

\( V(x) \) takes both positive and negative values in a given region.

Example: \( V(x) = x_1^2 - 2x_2^2 \)

4.3 Quadratic Form & Sylvester's Criterion

A common scalar function used in Lyapunov analysis is the quadratic form:

$$ V(x) = x^T P x $$

Where \( x \) is the state vector and \( P \) is a real symmetric matrix (\( P^T = P \)).

$$ V(x) = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 & x_2 & x_3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} P_{11} & P_{12} & P_{13} \\ P_{21} & P_{22} & P_{23} \\ P_{31} & P_{32} & P_{33} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \end{bmatrix} $$

Because \( P \) is symmetric, \( P_{ij} = P_{ji} \). This expands to:

$$ V(x) = P_{11}x_1^2 + P_{22}x_2^2 + P_{33}x_3^2 + 2P_{12}x_1x_2 + 2P_{13}x_1x_3 + 2P_{23}x_2x_3 $$

Sylvester's Criterion

To test the positive definiteness of \( V(x) = x^T P x \), we use Sylvester's Criterion on the matrix \( P \):

Rule: The quadratic form is Positive Definite if and only if all the successive principal minors of \( P \) are strictly positive (\( > 0 \)).

$$ \Delta_1 = P_{11} > 0 $$ $$ \Delta_2 = \begin{vmatrix} P_{11} & P_{12} \\ P_{21} & P_{22} \end{vmatrix} > 0 $$ $$ \Delta_3 = \begin{vmatrix} P_{11} & P_{12} & P_{13} \\ P_{21} & P_{22} & P_{23} \\ P_{31} & P_{32} & P_{33} \end{vmatrix} > 0 $$

4.4 Lyapunov's Second (Direct) Method

The Direct Method of Lyapunov is a powerful mathematical tool to determine the stability of nonlinear systems without needing to solve the differential equations.

Lyapunov's Stability Theorem

Let \( x=0 \) be an equilibrium point of \( \dot{x} = F(x) \). Let \( V(x) \) be a continuously differentiable scalar function.

  1. If \( V(x) \) is positive definite (\( V(x) > 0 \) for \( x \ne 0 \), and \( V(0)=0 \))...
  2. And if the time derivative \( \dot{V}(x) = \frac{dV(x)}{dt} \) along the trajectories of the system is negative semi-definite (\( \dot{V}(x) \le 0 \)), then the origin is stable.
  3. If \( \dot{V}(x) \) is strictly negative definite (\( \dot{V}(x) < 0 \) for \( x \ne 0 \)), then the origin is asymptotically stable.

How to compute \( \dot{V}(x) \):

$$ \dot{V}(x) = \frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{\partial V}{\partial x_1}\dot{x}_1 + \frac{\partial V}{\partial x_2}\dot{x}_2 + \dots + \frac{\partial V}{\partial x_n}\dot{x}_n $$

Problem 1: Checking Definiteness

Question: Determine if the given quadratic form is positive definite or not:
\( V(x) = 10x_1^2 + 4x_2^2 + x_3^2 + 2x_1x_2 - 2x_2x_3 - 4x_1x_3 \)

Step 1: Form the P Matrix

Compare with the standard quadratic form expansion: \( V(x) = P_{11}x_1^2 + P_{22}x_2^2 + P_{33}x_3^2 + 2P_{12}x_1x_2 + 2P_{13}x_1x_3 + 2P_{23}x_2x_3 \).

  • \( P_{11} = 10 \)
  • \( P_{22} = 4 \)
  • \( P_{33} = 1 \)
  • \( 2P_{12} = 2 \implies P_{12} = 1 \)
  • \( 2P_{13} = -4 \implies P_{13} = -2 \)
  • \( 2P_{23} = -2 \implies P_{23} = -1 \)

The symmetric matrix \( P \) is:

$$ P = \begin{bmatrix} 10 & 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 4 & -1 \\ -2 & -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Step 2: Apply Sylvester's Theorem

Calculate the leading principal minors:

$$ \Delta_1 = |10| = 10 > 0 $$
$$ \Delta_2 = \begin{vmatrix} 10 & 1 \\ 1 & 4 \end{vmatrix} = (10)(4) - (1)(1) = 40 - 1 = 39 > 0 $$
$$ \Delta_3 = |P| = \begin{vmatrix} 10 & 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 4 & -1 \\ -2 & -1 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = 10(4 - 1) - 1(1 - 2) - 2(-1 + 8) $$ $$ \Delta_3 = 10(3) - 1(-1) - 2(7) = 30 + 1 - 14 = 17 > 0 $$

All principal minors are greater than zero (\( \Delta_1, \Delta_2, \Delta_3 > 0 \)).
Hence, \( P \) is a positive definite matrix, and \( V(x) \) is a POSITIVE DEFINITE scalar function.

Problem 2: Lyapunov Equation for Linear Systems

Question: A second order system is represented by \( \dot{X} = AX \), where \( A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \). Assuming matrix \( Q \) to be an identity matrix, solve for matrix \( P \) in the Lyapunov equation \( A^T P + P A = -Q \). Determine stability and write the Lyapunov function \( V(x) \).

Theory Note:

For linear time-invariant systems \( \dot{x} = Ax \), we choose \( V(x) = x^T P x \). Taking the derivative:
\( \dot{V}(x) = \dot{x}^T P x + x^T P \dot{x} = x^T A^T P x + x^T P A x = x^T (A^T P + P A) x \).
To ensure \( \dot{V}(x) \) is negative definite (for asymptotic stability), we set \( A^T P + P A = -Q \), where \( Q \) is a positive definite matrix (often chosen as the Identity matrix \( I \)). If solving this yields a positive definite \( P \), the system is stable.

Step 1: Set up the Lyapunov Equation

$$ A^T P + P A = -Q $$ $$ \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} P_{11} & P_{12} \\ P_{12} & P_{22} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} P_{11} & P_{12} \\ P_{12} & P_{22} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & -1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Perform matrix multiplication:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} -P_{12} & -P_{22} \\ P_{11}-P_{12} & P_{12}-P_{22} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -P_{12} & P_{11}-P_{12} \\ -P_{22} & P_{12}-P_{22} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Add the matrices on the left:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} -2P_{12} & P_{11}-P_{12}-P_{22} \\ P_{11}-P_{12}-P_{22} & 2(P_{12}-P_{22}) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Step 2: Equate terms and solve for P

1) \( -2P_{12} = -1 \implies P_{12} = 0.5 \)

2) \( 2(P_{12} - P_{22}) = -1 \implies 2(0.5 - P_{22}) = -1 \implies 1 - 2P_{22} = -1 \implies 2P_{22} = 2 \implies P_{22} = 1 \)

3) \( P_{11} - P_{12} - P_{22} = 0 \implies P_{11} - 0.5 - 1 = 0 \implies P_{11} = 1.5 \)

Thus, the matrix P is:

$$ P = \begin{bmatrix} 1.5 & 0.5 \\ 0.5 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Step 3: Check Stability and write \( V(x) \)

Check positive definiteness of \( P \) using Sylvester's theorem:

$$ \Delta_1 = |1.5| = 1.5 > 0 $$ $$ \Delta_2 = \begin{vmatrix} 1.5 & 0.5 \\ 0.5 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = (1.5)(1) - (0.5)(0.5) = 1.5 - 0.25 = 1.25 > 0 $$

Since \( P \) is positive definite, the system is asymptotically stable in the large.

The Lyapunov function is:

$$ V(x) = x^T P x = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 & x_2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1.5 & 0.5 \\ 0.5 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} $$
$$ V(x) = 1.5x_1^2 + x_2^2 + x_1x_2 $$
