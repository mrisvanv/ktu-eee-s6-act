1 Types of Controllers & Compensation Schemes

Fundamental Concept May 2019

In industrial control systems, various control architectures are employed based on the requirement of disturbance rejection, stability, and process dynamics. Furthermore, compensators can be introduced into these systems using different topological schemes.

1.1 Series vs Feedback Compensation

When introducing a compensator \( G_c(s) \) into a control loop to meet desired specifications, two primary locations are commonly used:

Series (Cascade) Compensation

The compensator is placed directly in the forward path, in series with the plant.

  • Energy transfer is from lower to higher energy levels.
  • Requires additional amplifiers for isolation and gain recovery.
  • The most common and straightforward method for Lead/Lag design.

Feedback (Parallel) Compensation

The compensator is placed in a minor inner feedback loop around the plant or a portion of the plant.

  • Energy transfer is from higher to lower energy levels.
  • Often does not require additional amplifiers.
  • Provides robust control against plant parameter variations.

1.2 Feedback Controller

The idea behind the basic feedback controller is that the output of the controlled process (plant), also called the measured variable or the process variable, is compared to the set point or the desired value. The difference between the two is the error, which is the input to the controller. The controller generates an output based on this error so as to minimize the error signal to zero. The controller output is fed to the actuator which will then act on the plant.

Standard Feedback Control Loop

1.3 Cascade Controller

Cascade controller is the technique of nesting one control loop inside another. It allows the workload to be shared between the two loops. The name derives from the fact that the output of the outer controller is cascaded into the set point of the inner controller.

Example: Steam-fed Water Heater

Consider a tank where water is heated by steam. In a simple feedback loop, a temperature sensor measures tank water temperature and a single controller adjusts the steam valve. If the steam supply pressure drops, the controller won't know until the water temperature actually falls.

Cascade Solution: We use two controllers.

  • Controller 1 (Primary/Outer): Monitors tank temperature and outputs a desired steam flow rate (setpoint for Controller 2).
  • Controller 2 (Secondary/Inner): Monitors the actual steam flow rate and adjusts the steam valve.

Thus, if steam pressure drops, Controller 2 immediately opens the valve wider to maintain the required flow rate before the water temperature even begins to change.

Cascade Control Block Diagram

Advantages

  • The load of the control task is shared between the two controllers.
  • Control is faster and more accurate because inner loop corrects disturbances quickly.
  • Reduces the effect of nonlinearities in the secondary process.

Drawbacks

  • The extra sensor and the extra controller increases the overall cost.
  • The overall control system becomes complex.
  • Requires twice as much tuning effort (must tune secondary loop first, then primary).

1.4 Feedforward Controller

In this technique, the control action is based on the disturbance input into the process without considering the condition of the process output. In concept, feedforward control yields much faster correction than feedback control and is applied in such a way that the disturbance is ideally never seen in the controlled variable.

For example, if the inlet water temperature to a tank drops, a feedforward controller detects this incoming disturbance and immediately opens the steam valve to add more heat, perfectly offsetting the disturbance before the tank's overall temperature falls.

Limitations of Feedforward Control

  • It is usually very difficult to measure all possible disturbances accurately.
  • The controller will fail if the process model is inaccurate (it operates "blind" to the actual output).
  • Therefore, it is almost always combined with a feedback controller to handle unmeasured disturbances and modeling errors.

2 Basic Control Actions: P, I, PI, PID

VERY IMPORTANT Apr 2018May 2019Sep 2020May 2023

A controller computes a corrective signal \( u(t) \) based on the error \( e(t) \) (the difference between the desired setpoint \( r(t) \) and measured output \( c(t) \)). Standard industrial controllers use combinations of Proportional, Integral, and Derivative actions.

2.1 Proportional Controller (P-Controller)

The proportional controller produces a control signal \( u(t) \) that is strictly proportional to the error signal \( e(t) \).

Block Diagram of P-Control Action

$$u(t) = K_p e(t) \implies G_c(s) = K_p$$

Analysis of a P-Controller

Let the plant under consideration be a general first-order system: \( G(s) = \frac{K}{1+Ts} \)

The closed-loop transfer function is:

$$\frac{C(s)}{R(s)} = \frac{K_p \left( \frac{K}{1+Ts} \right)}{1 + K_p \left( \frac{K}{1+Ts} \right)} = \frac{K K_p}{1 + K K_p + Ts} = \left( \frac{K K_p}{1 + K K_p} \right) \frac{1}{1 + \frac{T s}{1 + K K_p}}$$

Let \( T_1 = \frac{T}{1 + K K_p} \) and \( K_1 = \frac{K K_p}{1 + K K_p} \).
The resulting system is a first-order system with a lower time constant (\( T_1 < T \)), which means P-control results in a faster response.

Steady State Error Analysis:

For a step input \( R(s) = A/s \):

$$E(s) = R(s) - C(s) = R(s) \left[ 1 - \frac{K K_p}{1 + K K_p + Ts} \right] = \frac{A}{s} \left[ \frac{1 + Ts}{1 + K K_p + Ts} \right]$$ $$e_{ss} = \lim_{s \to 0} s E(s) = \lim_{s \to 0} s \frac{A}{s} \left[ \frac{1 + Ts}{1 + K K_p + Ts} \right] = \frac{A}{1 + K K_p}$$

Conclusion for P-Control: As the value of \( K_p \) is increased, the steady state error (\( e_{ss} \)) decreases. However, it never reaches zero (there is always an offset). Furthermore, increasing \( K_p \) excessively for higher-order plants will cause large oscillations and eventually instability.

2.2 Integral Control (I-Controller)

In integral control, the output of the controller is proportional to the integral of the error signal. This means it accumulates past errors over time.

Block Diagram of I-Control Action

$$u(t) = K_i \int e(t) dt \implies G_c(s) = \frac{K_i}{s}$$

Analysis of an I-Controller

Let the plant be \( G(s) = \frac{K}{1+Ts} \).

The closed-loop transfer function with an Integral controller \( \frac{K_i}{s} \) is:

$$\frac{C(s)}{R(s)} = \frac{\frac{K_i}{s} \left( \frac{K}{1+Ts} \right)}{1 + \frac{K_i}{s} \left( \frac{K}{1+Ts} \right)} = \frac{K K_i}{s(1+Ts) + K K_i} = \frac{K K_i}{T s^2 + s + K K_i}$$

It is profoundly clear that the inclusion of an integral controller has increased the order of the closed-loop system by one (from a 1st order plant to a 2nd order closed-loop system). This increase in order may cause instability if the process is already of higher order dynamics.

Steady State Error Analysis:

For a step input \( R(s) = A/s \):

$$E(s) = R(s) - C(s) = R(s) \left[ 1 - \frac{K K_i}{T s^2 + s + K K_i} \right] = \frac{A}{s} \left[ \frac{T s^2 + s}{T s^2 + s + K K_i} \right]$$ $$e_{ss} = \lim_{s \to 0} s E(s) = \lim_{s \to 0} s \cdot \frac{A}{s} \left[ \frac{T s^2 + s}{T s^2 + s + K K_i} \right] = 0$$

Conclusion for I-Control: The major advantage of the integral action is that the steady state error due to step input is exactly zero. However, the system response becomes generally slow and oscillatory.

2.3 Proportional-Integral Controller (PI Controller)

The PI controller provides the dual advantage of fast response due to P-action and zero steady-state error due to I-action.

Block Diagram of PI-Controller

Here the control signal is given by:

$$u(t) = K_p e(t) + \frac{K_p}{K_i} \int e(t) dt$$ $$G_c(s) = K_p \left( 1 + \frac{1}{K_i s} \right)$$

2.4 Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) Controller

PID control combines the features of each of the three individual control actions to provide the optimal overall performance.

Block Diagram of PID-Controller

Here the control signal is given by:

$$u(t) = K_p e(t) + \frac{K_p}{K_i} \int e(t) dt + K_p K_d \frac{d}{dt}e(t)$$ $$G_c(s) = K_p \left( 1 + \frac{1}{K_i s} + K_d s \right)$$

Summary of PID Features

  • P-control: Improves the speed of response (reduces rise time).
  • I-control: Reduces or eliminates the steady-state error completely.
  • D-control: Reduces the rate of change of error, improving damping and reducing peak overshoot.

3 Compensator Analysis & Realization

MOST IMPORTANT (10 MARKS) Apr 2018May 2019Dec 2019Sep 2020Jul 2021

Compensators are corrective subsystems introduced to compensate for plant deficiencies. We will deeply analyze the Lead, Lag, and Lag-Lead compensators in the frequency domain, including their electrical RC realizations, transfer functions, pole-zero plots, and Bode plots.

HIGH-PASS FILTER

3.1 Lead Compensator

For a sinusoidal input, if the sinusoidal output leads the input, it is known as a lead compensator. It essentially acts as a High-Pass filter.

Primary Function: It is used to improve the transient response (speed up the system, decrease overshoot) and increase the stability margins (specifically, it increases Phase Margin).

Electrical RC Realization

Figure: Realization of Lead Compensator

Transfer Function Derivation

Using voltage divider rule:

$$\frac{E_o(s)}{E_i(s)} = \frac{R_2}{R_2 + \left( \frac{R_1 \cdot \frac{1}{Cs}}{R_1 + \frac{1}{Cs}} \right)}$$

Simplifying the expression yields:

$$G_c(s) = \frac{E_o(s)}{E_i(s)} = \frac{R_2 (R_1 Cs + 1)}{R_1 R_2 Cs + R_2 + R_1}$$

Let \( T = R_1 C \) and \( \alpha = \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \):

$$G_c(s) = \alpha \left( \frac{Ts + 1}{\alpha Ts + 1} \right) = \frac{s + \frac{1}{T}}{s + \frac{1}{\alpha T}}$$

Since \( R_1 \) and \( R_2 \) are positive, \( 0 < \alpha < 1 \).

Pole-Zero Plot

The lead compensator has a dominant zero at \(-1/T\) and a pole at \(-1/\alpha T\). Because \( \alpha < 1 \), the pole is located further to the left (farther from the origin) than the zero.

Frequency Response (Bode Plot)

Derivation of Maximum Phase Lead Angle (\(\phi_m\))

Let us evaluate the sinusoidal transfer function of the lead compensator (assuming the attenuation \( \alpha \) is compensated by an amplifier, giving unit DC gain):

$$G_c(j\omega) = \frac{j\omega T + 1}{j\alpha \omega T + 1}$$

The phase angle \(\phi\) is given by:

$$\phi = \angle G_c(j\omega) = \tan^{-1}(\omega T) - \tan^{-1}(\alpha \omega T)$$

To find the maximum phase angle \(\phi_m\), we take the derivative with respect to \(\omega\) and equate it to zero:

$$\frac{d\phi}{d\omega} = \frac{T}{1 + \omega^2 T^2} - \frac{\alpha T}{1 + \alpha^2 \omega^2 T^2} = 0$$ $$\frac{T}{1 + \omega^2 T^2} = \frac{\alpha T}{1 + \alpha^2 \omega^2 T^2} \implies 1 + \alpha^2 \omega^2 T^2 = \alpha + \alpha \omega^2 T^2$$ $$\omega^2 T^2 (\alpha^2 - \alpha) = \alpha - 1 \implies \omega^2 T^2 \alpha(\alpha - 1) = -(1 - \alpha)$$ $$\omega^2 = \frac{1}{\alpha T^2}$$

This gives the frequency \(\omega_m\) at which maximum phase lead occurs (the geometric mean of the two corner frequencies):

$$\omega_m = \frac{1}{T\sqrt{\alpha}}$$

Substituting \(\omega_m\) back into the phase equation to find \( \sin \phi_m \):

$$\tan \phi_m = \frac{\omega_m T - \alpha \omega_m T}{1 + \alpha \omega_m^2 T^2} = \frac{\frac{T}{T\sqrt{\alpha}} - \frac{\alpha T}{T\sqrt{\alpha}}}{1 + \alpha \left(\frac{1}{\alpha T^2}\right) T^2} = \frac{\frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha}} - \sqrt{\alpha}}{1 + 1} = \frac{1 - \alpha}{2\sqrt{\alpha}}$$

Using trigonometric identities (\( \sin\theta = \frac{\tan\theta}{\sqrt{1+\tan^2\theta}} \)):

$$\sin \phi_m = \frac{1 - \alpha}{1 + \alpha} \implies \alpha = \frac{1 - \sin \phi_m}{1 + \sin \phi_m}$$
LOW-PASS FILTER

3.2 Lag Compensator

For a sinusoidal input, if the sinusoidal output lags the input, it is known as a lag compensator. It essentially acts as a Low-Pass filter.

Primary Function: It is used to provide high gain at low frequencies to improve steady-state performance (reduce steady-state error). It accomplishes this through its high-frequency attenuation property, which allows the crossover frequency to be shifted to a lower value where the uncompensated system has a sufficient phase margin.

Electrical RC Realization

Figure: Realization of Lag Compensator

Transfer Function Derivation

Using voltage divider rule:

$$\frac{E_o(s)}{E_i(s)} = \frac{R_2 + \frac{1}{Cs}}{R_1 + R_2 + \frac{1}{Cs}}$$

Simplifying the expression yields:

$$G_c(s) = \frac{E_o(s)}{E_i(s)} = \frac{R_2 Cs + 1}{(R_1 + R_2)Cs + 1}$$

Let \( T = R_2 C \) and \( \beta = \frac{R_1 + R_2}{R_2} \):

$$G_c(s) = \frac{Ts + 1}{\beta Ts + 1} = \frac{1}{\beta} \left( \frac{s + \frac{1}{T}}{s + \frac{1}{\beta T}} \right)$$

Since \( R_1 \) and \( R_2 \) are positive, \( \beta > 1 \).

Pole-Zero Plot

The lag compensator has a zero at \(-1/T\) and a dominant pole at \(-1/\beta T\). Because \( \beta > 1 \), the pole is located closer to the origin than the zero.

Frequency Response (Bode Plot)

High Frequency Attenuation Analysis

Unlike the Lead Compensator, which is primarily used for its phase addition property, the Lag Compensator is primarily used for its high-frequency attenuation property. This allows us to increase the low-frequency gain (which improves steady-state error) without destroying the high-frequency stability margins.

The magnitude of the lag compensator at high frequencies (\(\omega \to \infty\)) is:

$$|G_c(j\omega)|_{\omega \to \infty} = \left| \frac{j\omega T + 1}{j\beta\omega T + 1} \right| \approx \left| \frac{j\omega T}{j\beta\omega T} \right| = \frac{1}{\beta}$$

Expressed in decibels, the high-frequency attenuation is:

$$\text{Attenuation} = 20 \log_{10} \left(\frac{1}{\beta}\right) = -20 \log_{10} \beta \text{ dB}$$

Similar to the lead compensator, the frequency at which maximum phase lag occurs is the geometric mean of the corner frequencies:

$$\omega_m = \frac{1}{T\sqrt{\beta}}$$

And the maximum phase lag angle is given by:

$$\sin \phi_m = \frac{1 - \beta}{1 + \beta}$$
BAND-REJECT / DUAL

3.3 Lag-Lead Compensator

In a lag-lead network, when a sinusoidal signal is applied, both phase lag and phase lead occur in the output, but in different frequency regions. Phase lag occurs in the low-frequency region (improving steady-state error) and phase lead occurs in the high-frequency region (improving transient response and stability).

Electrical RC Realization

Figure: Realization of Lag-Lead Compensator

Transfer Function Derivation

The circuit is a voltage divider with series impedance \( Z_1 \) and shunt impedance \( Z_2 \):

$$Z_1 = \frac{R_1 \frac{1}{C_1 s}}{R_1 + \frac{1}{C_1 s}} = \frac{R_1}{R_1 C_1 s + 1}$$ $$Z_2 = R_2 + \frac{1}{C_2 s} = \frac{R_2 C_2 s + 1}{C_2 s}$$

The transfer function is:

$$G_c(s) = \frac{E_o(s)}{E_i(s)} = \frac{Z_2}{Z_1 + Z_2}$$

Substituting and simplifying yields:

$$G_c(s) = \frac{(R_1 C_1 s + 1)(R_2 C_2 s + 1)}{(R_1 C_1 s + 1)(R_2 C_2 s + 1) + R_1 C_2 s}$$

Standard Form Translation

Let \( T_1 = R_1 C_1 \) and \( T_2 = R_2 C_2 \). The transfer function can be expressed in the standard pole-zero form:

$$G_c(s) = K_c \frac{\left(s + \frac{1}{T_1}\right)\left(s + \frac{1}{T_2}\right)}{\left(s + \frac{1}{\beta T_1}\right)\left(s + \frac{1}{\alpha T_2}\right)}$$

By matching the coefficients of the actual RC network to the standard form, we establish the critical relationship for a passive lag-lead network:

$$\alpha \beta = 1 \implies \alpha = \frac{1}{\beta}$$

Where the first term \( \left( \frac{s + \frac{1}{T_1}}{s + \frac{1}{\beta T_1}} \right) \) provides the Lag effect (\(\beta > 1\)), and the second term \( \left( \frac{s + \frac{1}{T_2}}{s + \frac{1}{\alpha T_2}} \right) \) provides the Lead effect (\(\alpha < 1\)).

Pole-Zero Plot

Contains two zeros and two poles. The Lag pole-zero pair is closer to the origin. The Lead pole-zero pair is further out.

Frequency Response (Bode Plot)

Shows characteristic magnitude dip (lag) and subsequent phase swing from negative to positive.

Figure: Bode plot of lag-lead compensator

4 Step-by-Step Bode Design

FREQUENTLY ASKED ESSAY Apr 2018Dec 2019Jul 2021

The most common university examination questions involve designing Lead or Lag compensators using Bode plot techniques. Below are the definitive, step-by-step algorithms required to solve these numerical problems.

Lead Compensator Design

1

Determine open-loop gain \( K \)

Determine the value of the open-loop gain \( K \) in order to satisfy the given requirement on the static error constant (e.g., \( K_v = \lim_{s \to 0} sG(s) \)).

2

Evaluate initial Phase Margin

Using the gain \( K \) determined above, draw the Bode diagram of the gain-adjusted uncompensated system and evaluate the existing Phase Margin (\( PM_{system} \)).

3

Calculate required phase lead \( \phi_m \)

Determine the necessary phase lead angle to be added to the system:

$$\phi_m = PM_{desired} - PM_{system} + \epsilon$$

Add an additional \( \epsilon \approx 5^\circ \) to \( 12^\circ \) because the addition of the lead compensator shifts the gain crossover frequency to the right, which generally decreases the phase margin.

4

Calculate attenuation factor \( \alpha \)

$$\alpha = \frac{1 - \sin\phi_m}{1 + \sin\phi_m}$$
5

Find new gain crossover \( \omega_m \)

Determine the frequency where the magnitude of the uncompensated system is exactly equal to:

$$-20 \log_{10} \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha}}\right)$$

Select this frequency as the new gain crossover frequency \( \omega_m \).

6

Calculate \( T \) and finalize

Knowing that \( \omega_m = 1/(T\sqrt{\alpha}) \), calculate \( T \):

$$T = \frac{1}{\omega_m\sqrt{\alpha}}$$

Determine the transfer function of the lead compensator:

$$G_c(s) = \frac{\alpha(1 + sT)}{1 + \alpha sT}$$

Finally, verify the design by checking the phase margin of the overall compensated system \( G_c(s)G(s) \).

Lag Compensator Design

1

Determine open-loop gain \( K \)

Determine the value of the open-loop gain \( K \) in order to satisfy the given requirement on the static error constant.

2

Draw uncompensated Bode Plot

Using the gain \( K \) determined above, draw the Bode diagram of the gain-adjusted system and evaluate the phase margin.

3

Find new gain crossover \( \omega_{gcn} \)

Find the frequency at which the phase angle of the uncompensated system is equal to:

$$-180^\circ + PM_{desired} + \epsilon$$

Add 5° to 12° to compensate for the slight phase lag contributed by the lag compensator at the new crossover. This frequency becomes the new gain crossover frequency \( \omega_{gcn} \).

4

Place the zero \( 1/T \)

To prevent detrimental effects of phase lag, the pole and zero must be located substantially lower than the new crossover frequency. Choose the corner frequency (zero) 1 decade below the new gain crossover:

$$z_c = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{\omega_{gcn}}{10}$$
5

Calculate attenuation \( \beta \)

Determine the attenuation necessary to bring the magnitude down to 0 dB at the new gain crossover frequency. Measure the uncompensated magnitude at \( \omega_{gcn} \) (let's call it \( M \) in dB).

$$M = 20 \log_{10} \beta \implies \beta = 10^{\frac{M}{20}}$$
6

Calculate Pole and finalize

The pole is located at \( 1/(\beta T) \). Determine the transfer function:

$$G_c(s) = \frac{1 + sT}{1 + s\beta T}$$

Verify the design by checking the phase margin of the overall compensated system.
